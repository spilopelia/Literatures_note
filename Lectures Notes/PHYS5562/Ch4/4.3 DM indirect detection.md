## 1) What “indirect detection” means
Indirect detection looks for **Standard Model particles produced by dark matter** (DM) interactions (annihilation or decay) far away, instead of trying to hit DM in a lab. :contentReference[oaicite:1]{index=1}

## 2) DM annihilation rate in a volume element

### 2.1 Convert mass density to number density
If DM has mass density “rho” and particle mass “m”, the number density is
$$
n_\chi=\frac{\rho_\chi}{m_\chi}.
$$

### 2.2 “Self-conjugate” DM (DM is its own antiparticle; e.g. Majorana-like)
The annihilation rate in a small volume element is written in the slides as :contentReference[oaicite:2]{index=2}
$$
d\Gamma_{\rm ann}=\frac{1}{2}\left(\frac{\rho_\chi}{m_\chi}\right)^2 \langle\sigma v\rangle \, dV.
$$

**Why the factor 1/2? (double counting derivation)**
- In volume \(dV\), the number of particles is \(N=n_\chi\, dV\).
- The number of *distinct pairs* you can form is
$$
N_{\rm pairs}=\frac{N(N-1)}{2}\simeq \frac{N^2}{2}\quad (N\gg 1).
$$
- The microscopic annihilation probability per pair per time is \(\langle\sigma v\rangle / dV\)-type scaling, so the macroscopic rate becomes proportional to \(N_{\rm pairs}\langle\sigma v\rangle\).
- That produces the overall
$$
\frac{1}{2}\,n_\chi^2\langle\sigma v\rangle dV
$$
which is exactly the slide expression.

### 2.3 “Symmetric DM” (equal DM and anti-DM; e.g. Dirac particle + antiparticle)
The slides state that if DM is symmetric (equal particle and antiparticle), the rate becomes :contentReference[oaicite:3]{index=3}
$$
d\Gamma_{\rm ann}=\left(\frac{1}{2}\frac{\rho_\chi}{m_\chi}\right)^2\langle\sigma v\rangle\, dV.
$$

**Why does symmetric DM introduce an *additional* 1/2 (compared to self-conjugate)?**
- For Dirac-like DM, annihilation requires **one \(\chi\)** and **one \(\bar\chi\)**, so
$$
d\Gamma_{\rm ann}=\langle\sigma v\rangle\, n_\chi n_{\bar\chi}\, dV.
$$
- If the *total* DM mass density is \(\rho_\chi\) and it is split equally,
$$
\rho_\chi=\rho_\chi^{(\chi)}+\rho_\chi^{(\bar\chi)},\qquad \rho_\chi^{(\chi)}=\rho_\chi^{(\bar\chi)}=\frac{\rho_\chi}{2}.
$$
Then
$$
n_\chi=n_{\bar\chi}=\frac{\rho_\chi/2}{m_\chi}=\frac{1}{2}\frac{\rho_\chi}{m_\chi}.
$$
So
$$
d\Gamma_{\rm ann}=\langle\sigma v\rangle\left(\frac{1}{2}\frac{\rho_\chi}{m_\chi}\right)^2 dV,
$$
which matches the slide.
- Compare to self-conjugate DM:
  - self-conjugate: \(\frac{1}{2}\left(\frac{\rho}{m}\right)^2\)
  - symmetric: \(\left(\frac{1}{2}\frac{\rho}{m}\right)^2=\frac{1}{4}\left(\frac{\rho}{m}\right)^2\)

So symmetric DM is smaller by a factor
$$
\frac{(1/4)}{(1/2)}=\frac{1}{2}
$$
relative to self-conjugate DM, because (i) you only annihilate \(\chi\bar\chi\) pairs, and (ii) each species has half the density.

## 3) Annihilation channels and spectra (what we actually observe)
Per annihilation, you produce a spectrum in each messenger \(i\):
$$
\frac{dN_i}{dE}.
$$
Different “channels” (e.g. \(b\bar b\)) contribute with branching fractions; slides summarize this as :contentReference[oaicite:4]{index=4}
$$
\langle\sigma v\rangle=\langle\sigma v\rangle\sum_i {\rm Br}_i,
$$
i.e. total cross section is distributed across channels via \({\rm Br}_i\).

---

## 4) s-wave vs p-wave (velocity dependence of annihilation)
For non-relativistic annihilation, the slides parametrize :contentReference[oaicite:5]{index=5}
$$
\sigma v \simeq a + b v^2.
$$
- “s-wave” term: \(a\) (velocity-independent).
- “p-wave” term: \(b v^2\) (suppressed at low velocity).

If present-day halo velocities are \(v\sim 10^{-3}c\), then
$$
v^2\sim 10^{-6},
$$
so p-wave signals today are strongly suppressed, which is why “most people only consider s-wave for realistic detection.” :contentReference[oaicite:6]{index=6}

## 5) From annihilation rate to an observed flux (photons / neutrinos)

### 5.1 Emissivity from a small volume element
Combine “rate” and “spectrum per annihilation” to define a production rate per energy:
$$
d\dot N(E)=d\Gamma_{\rm ann}\,\frac{dN}{dE}.
$$
Using the slide definition (they call it \(\epsilon\)) :contentReference[oaicite:7]{index=7}
$$
\epsilon \equiv \frac{1}{2}\left(\frac{\rho_\chi}{m_\chi}\right)^2\langle\sigma v\rangle\, dV \,\frac{dN}{dE}.
$$

### 5.2 Geometric dilution to flux
If the volume element is at distance \(r\), the differential flux received at the observer is
$$
\frac{dF}{dE}=\frac{1}{4\pi r^2}\,\epsilon
=\frac{1}{2}\left(\frac{\rho_\chi}{m_\chi}\right)^2\langle\sigma v\rangle\, dV \,\frac{dN}{dE}\,\frac{1}{4\pi r^2}.
$$
This is the “More precisely” equation in the slides. :contentReference[oaicite:8]{index=8}

---

## 6) Two key geometries: (A) far & small source vs (B) extended Galactic halo

### 6.1 Case A — “very small and far away” (point-like approximation)
If the whole source is concentrated at essentially one distance \(r=D\), then \(1/(4\pi r^2)\) is nearly constant across the object, so you can pull it (and often \(dN/dE\)) outside the integral: :contentReference[oaicite:9]{index=9}
$$
\frac{dF}{dE}\simeq \frac{1}{4\pi D^2}\frac{dN}{dE}\int \frac{1}{2}\left(\frac{\rho_\chi}{m_\chi}\right)^2\langle\sigma v\rangle\, dV.
$$

**Interpretation**
- The integral is essentially the **intrinsic luminosity in annihilations** (weighted by \(\rho^2\)).
- The \(1/D^2\) is the usual inverse-square law.

### 6.2 Case B — Galactic halo annihilation (extended source; line-of-sight integral)
For the Milky Way halo, different emitting points have different distances to us, so the “small and far away” assumption fails. :contentReference[oaicite:10]{index=10}

Use line-of-sight coordinates:
- \(\ell\) = distance along the line of sight
- \(d\Omega\) = observed solid angle element on the sky
- Volume element:
$$
dV=\ell^2\, d\ell\, d\Omega.
$$
The slides rewrite the flux element as :contentReference[oaicite:11]{index=11}
$$
\frac{dF}{dE}
=\frac{1}{2}\left(\frac{\rho_\chi}{m_\chi}\right)^2\langle\sigma v\rangle
\left(\ell^2 d\ell d\Omega\right)\frac{dN}{dE}\frac{1}{4\pi \ell^2}.
$$

Now the key simplification (the “cancellation” you were asking about):
$$
\left(\ell^2 d\ell d\Omega\right)\times \frac{1}{\ell^2}=d\ell\, d\Omega.
$$
So
$$
\frac{dF}{dE}
=\frac{\langle\sigma v\rangle}{8\pi m_\chi^2}\frac{dN}{dE}\int d\ell\, d\Omega\;\rho_\chi^2(\ell,\Omega).
$$
This is exactly what the slides show. :contentReference[oaicite:12]{index=12}

And the flux **per solid angle** becomes
$$
\frac{dF}{dE\,d\Omega}
=\frac{\langle\sigma v\rangle}{8\pi m_\chi^2}\frac{dN}{dE}\int d\ell\;\rho_\chi^2(\ell,\Omega).
$$
:contentReference[oaicite:13]{index=13}

### 6.3 Geometry relation for \(\rho(r)\) in the Milky Way
Assuming spherical symmetry \(\rho=\rho(r)\), the Galactocentric radius \(r\) is related to \(\ell\) and the angle \(\psi\) from the Galactic Center by :contentReference[oaicite:14]{index=14}
$$
r^2(\ell,\psi)=R_\odot^2+\ell^2-2R_\odot\ell\cos\psi.
$$

## 7) The J-factor
The slides identify the astrophysical integral as the “J-factor” :contentReference[oaicite:15]{index=15}:
- For an ROI (region of interest) covering solid angle \(\Delta\Omega\),
$$
J(\Delta\Omega)=\int_{\Delta\Omega} d\Omega\int d\ell\;\rho_\chi^2(\ell,\Omega).
$$
Then
$$
\frac{dF}{dE}
=\frac{\langle\sigma v\rangle}{8\pi m_\chi^2}\frac{dN}{dE}\;J(\Delta\Omega).
$$
:contentReference[oaicite:16]{index=16}

---

## 8) “If distance cancels, why do far sources look dim?” (the confusion we discussed)
What cancels in the Galactic-halo derivation is the \(\ell^2\) from:
- geometric dilution \(1/(4\pi \ell^2)\), and
- the Jacobian in the volume element \(dV=\ell^2 d\ell d\Omega\).

That cancellation happens **for the intensity** (flux per solid angle):
$$
\frac{dF}{dE\,d\Omega}\propto \int d\ell\,\rho^2,
$$
so **surface brightness** does not explicitly carry a \(1/D^2\) factor.

But the **total flux from a bounded object** still scales like \(1/D^2\), because the object’s apparent solid angle shrinks with distance:
- If an object has physical size \(R\), then
$$
\Delta\Omega \sim \pi\left(\frac{R}{D}\right)^2.
$$
- So even if \(dF/(dE\,d\Omega)\) is “distance-independent-ish”, integrating over \(\Delta\Omega\) gives back roughly \(1/D^2\).

**Rule of thumb**
- Use the “far & small” form when the source is effectively point-like in your analysis window.
- Use the line-of-sight/J-factor form when the source is extended (Milky Way halo, Galactic Center region, diffuse emission).

## 9) Extension: DM decay (contrast with annihilation)
The slides explicitly contrast annihilation vs decay: annihilation scales as \(\rho^2\), decay as \(\rho\). :contentReference[oaicite:17]{index=17}

If decay rate is
$$
\Gamma=\frac{1}{\tau},
$$
then production in volume element is :contentReference[oaicite:18]{index=18}
$$
d\Gamma_{\rm dec}=\left(\frac{\rho_\chi}{m_\chi}\right)\Gamma\, dV,
$$
and the decay flux becomes
$$
\frac{dF}{dE\,d\Omega}
=\frac{\Gamma}{4\pi m_\chi}\frac{dN}{dE}\int d\ell\;\rho_\chi(\ell,\Omega).
$$
:contentReference[oaicite:19]{index=19}

## 10) Tracking electrons/positrons: why the flux formula changes

### 10.1 Key physical difference: photons/neutrinos vs e±
- Photons/neutrinos: travel nearly straight, so flux is basically “production × 1/(4πr²)”.
- Electrons/positrons: **diffuse in magnetic fields** and **lose energy** (synchrotron, inverse Compton, bremsstrahlung), so the observed spectrum at Earth is **not** a simple line-of-sight integral.

The slides say: for e± from DM annihilation/decay, treat it by writing the correct **production term** in the propagation equation. :contentReference[oaicite:20]{index=20}

### 10.2 Source term (production term) from DM
For annihilation, the injection (per volume, per energy, per time) is
$$
Q_{\rm ann}(\vec r,E)=\frac{1}{2}\left(\frac{\rho_\chi(\vec r)}{m_\chi}\right)^2\langle\sigma v\rangle\;\frac{dN_{e^\pm}}{dE}.
$$
(And sum over channels using branching fractions if needed.)

For decay,
$$
Q_{\rm dec}(\vec r,E)=\left(\frac{\rho_\chi(\vec r)}{m_\chi}\right)\Gamma\;\frac{dN_{e^\pm}}{dE}.
$$

### 10.3 “Propagation equation of cosmic rays” — term-by-term mapping to your full equation
You wrote the standard CR transport equation:
$$
\frac{\partial\psi(\vec{r}, p, t)}{\partial t}
= q(\vec{r}, p, t)
+ \vec{\nabla} \cdot (D_{xx}\vec{\nabla}\psi - \vec{V}\psi)
+ \frac{\partial}{\partial p} \left[ p^2 D_{pp} \frac{\partial}{\partial p} \frac{\psi}{p^2} \right]
- \frac{\partial}{\partial p} \left[ \dot{p}\psi - \frac{p}{3}(\vec{\nabla}\cdot\vec{V})\psi \right]
- \frac{1}{\tau_f}\psi
- \frac{1}{\tau_r}\psi.
$$

Here is what each term means, and how it matches the simpler “diffusion-loss” form often used for e±:

#### (1) Time evolution
$$
\frac{\partial\psi}{\partial t}
$$
How the phase-space density changes with time.

- For many DM e± applications one assumes steady state:
$$
\frac{\partial\psi}{\partial t}=0.
$$

#### (2) Source / injection
$$
q(\vec r,p,t)
$$
This is exactly where DM annihilation/decay enters:
$$
q \leftrightarrow Q_{\rm ann/dec}.
$$

#### (3) Spatial diffusion and convection (bulk wind)
$$
\vec{\nabla}\cdot(D_{xx}\vec{\nabla}\psi)
\quad\text{and}\quad
-\vec{\nabla}\cdot(\vec V\psi)
$$
- \(D_{xx}\): diffusion coefficient in space (random walk in turbulent magnetic fields).
- \(\vec V\): convective wind (galactic outflow), advects particles away.

A common “diffusion-loss” simplification for local e± often drops convection:
$$
\vec V\simeq 0.
$$

#### (4) Diffusion in momentum (“reacceleration”)
$$
\frac{\partial}{\partial p}\left[p^2D_{pp}\frac{\partial}{\partial p}\left(\frac{\psi}{p^2}\right)\right]
$$
- \(D_{pp}\): momentum-space diffusion coefficient.
- Physically: second-order Fermi acceleration on moving magnetic turbulence.

Often neglected in minimal analytic treatments, kept in numerical codes (GALPROP/DRAGON).

#### (5) Continuous momentum/energy losses + adiabatic term
$$
-\frac{\partial}{\partial p}\left[\dot p\,\psi\right]
\quad\text{and}\quad
-\frac{\partial}{\partial p}\left[-\frac{p}{3}(\nabla\cdot \vec V)\psi\right]
$$
- \(\dot p\): continuous momentum losses (for e± dominated by radiative losses; in energy language this becomes \(b(E)\)).
- The \((\nabla\cdot\vec V)\) piece is adiabatic energy change from expanding/contracting flow.

For high-energy e± in the ISM, radiative losses dominate, giving a compact energy-loss operator:
$$
\frac{\partial}{\partial E}\left[b(E)\,\psi(\vec r,E)\right].
$$

#### (6) Catastrophic losses: fragmentation and radioactive decay
$$
-\frac{\psi}{\tau_f}-\frac{\psi}{\tau_r}
$$
Important for nuclei; for electrons/positrons these are usually not the leading terms (radiative losses are).

### 10.4 The “diffusion–loss equation” for e± from DM (common reduced form)
If you:
- set steady state,
- neglect reacceleration (\(D_{pp}\to 0\)),
- neglect convection (\(\vec V\to 0\)),
- neglect catastrophic losses (\(\tau_f,\tau_r\to \infty\)),

then the transport equation reduces to
$$
0 = Q(\vec r,E) + \vec\nabla\cdot\!\left(D(E)\,\vec\nabla\psi(\vec r,E)\right)
+ \frac{\partial}{\partial E}\!\left(b(E)\,\psi(\vec r,E)\right).
$$

This is the core reason the “flux equation” changes for e±: the observed \(\psi\) at Earth is the solution of this PDE, not a direct line-of-sight integral.

### 10.5 How the observed e± flux is obtained
Once you solve for \(\psi\) at the Solar position \(\vec r_\odot\), the differential flux is
$$
\Phi_{e^\pm}(E)=\frac{v_{e^\pm}}{4\pi}\,\psi(\vec r_\odot,E)
\simeq \frac{c}{4\pi}\,\psi(\vec r_\odot,E).
$$

So the steps are:
1. Build \(Q(\vec r,E)\) from DM (\(\rho^2\) for annihilation, \(\rho\) for decay).
2. Solve propagation equation to get \(\psi(\vec r_\odot,E)\).
3. Convert \(\psi\to\Phi\).

## 11) Quick checklist of assumptions to state in exam answers
- DM distribution model: spherical halo, \(\rho=\rho(r)\) (e.g. NFW) :contentReference[oaicite:21]{index=21}
- Annihilation is s-wave unless stated otherwise (p-wave suppressed today) :contentReference[oaicite:22]{index=22}
- Far/small source: treat distance constant; extended source: use line-of-sight + J-factor :contentReference[oaicite:23]{index=23}
- Photons/neutrinos: straight-line propagation; e±: diffusion + losses (use transport equation) :contentReference[oaicite:24]{index=24}
