# Solar neutrinos (Lecture 3.2) — Obsidian exam revision note

## 0) Big picture
Solar neutrinos are neutrinos produced inside the Sun.

Two broad categories:
- **Solar nuclear neutrinos**: produced by fusion reactions powering the Sun (detected).
- **Solar thermal neutrinos**: produced by thermal processes in the hot plasma (very low energy; historically not the main detected signal).

---

## 1) Standard Solar Model (SSM): equations of stellar structure
Assumptions (for these equations):
- Spherical symmetry: all quantities depend only on radius.
- Quasi-static equilibrium (Sun evolves slowly compared to dynamical timescales).
- Local thermodynamic equilibrium (for defining T, P, etc.).

### 1.1 Mass conservation
**Result**
$$
\frac{dm}{dr}=4\pi r^2\rho(r)
$$

**Derivation**
1) Consider a thin spherical shell at radius r with thickness dr.  
2) Shell volume:
$$
dV = 4\pi r^2\,dr
$$
3) Shell mass:
$$
dm = \rho(r)\,dV = \rho(r)\,4\pi r^2\,dr
$$
4) Divide by dr:
$$
\frac{dm}{dr}=4\pi r^2\rho(r)
$$

**Meaning of terms**
- m(r): mass enclosed within radius r
- r: distance from solar center
- ρ(r): mass density
- 4πr²: surface area of a sphere

---

### 1.2 Hydrostatic equilibrium
**Result**
$$
\frac{dP}{dr}=-\frac{Gm(r)}{r^2}\rho(r)
$$

**Derivation**
1) Take a thin shell with thickness dr and area:
$$
A = 4\pi r^2
$$
2) Pressure at inner face is P(r). Pressure at outer face is:
$$
P(r+dr)=P(r)+\frac{dP}{dr}\,dr
$$
3) Net pressure force on the shell (inward direction is positive here):
$$
dF_P = P(r)A - P(r+dr)A = -\frac{dP}{dr}\,dr\,A
$$
4) Shell mass:
$$
dm = \rho(r)\,A\,dr
$$
5) Gravitational force on shell due to enclosed mass m(r):
$$
dF_g = \frac{Gm(r)\,dm}{r^2}=\frac{Gm(r)}{r^2}\rho(r)\,A\,dr
$$
6) Hydrostatic equilibrium means pressure gradient balances gravity:
$$
dF_P=dF_g
$$
7) Substitute and cancel A dr:
$$
-\frac{dP}{dr}=\frac{Gm(r)}{r^2}\rho(r)
$$
8) Rearranged:
$$
\frac{dP}{dr}=-\frac{Gm(r)}{r^2}\rho(r)
$$

**Meaning of terms**
- P(r): pressure
- G: gravitational constant
- m(r)/r²: gravitational pull at radius r from interior mass
- ρ(r): density “feeling” gravity
- Negative sign: pressure decreases outward

---

### 1.3 Energy generation: luminosity growth with radius
**Result**
$$
\frac{dL}{dr}=4\pi r^2\rho(r)\,q(r)
$$

**Derivation**
1) Define q(r) as energy produced per unit mass per unit time (specific power).  
2) Energy produced per unit time in a shell:
$$
dL = q(r)\,dm
$$
3) Use shell mass:
$$
dm=\rho(r)\,4\pi r^2dr
$$
4) Then:
$$
dL = q(r)\rho(r)\,4\pi r^2dr
$$
5) Divide by dr:
$$
\frac{dL}{dr}=4\pi r^2\rho(r)\,q(r)
$$

**Meaning of terms**
- L(r): luminosity passing through radius r
- q(r): energy generation rate per unit mass (from fusion)
- ρq: energy generation per unit volume

---

### 1.4 Radiative transport: temperature gradient for photon diffusion
**Result**
$$
\frac{dT}{dr}=-\frac{3}{4ac}\frac{\kappa\rho}{T^3}\frac{L}{4\pi r^2}
$$

**Derivation**
1) In the diffusion approximation, radiative flux F is:
$$
F=-D\frac{du}{dr}
$$
2) Radiation energy density u relates to temperature:
$$
u=aT^4
$$
3) Differentiate u:
$$
\frac{du}{dr}=4aT^3\frac{dT}{dr}
$$
4) Diffusion coefficient for photons in matter:
$$
D=\frac{c}{3\kappa\rho}
$$
5) Substitute into flux:
$$
F=-\frac{c}{3\kappa\rho}\,4aT^3\frac{dT}{dr}
$$
6) Relate flux to luminosity:
$$
F=\frac{L}{4\pi r^2}
$$
7) Set equal and solve for dT/dr:
$$
\frac{L}{4\pi r^2}=-\frac{4acT^3}{3\kappa\rho}\frac{dT}{dr}
$$
$$
\frac{dT}{dr}=-\frac{3}{4ac}\frac{\kappa\rho}{T^3}\frac{L}{4\pi r^2}
$$

**Meaning of terms**
- T(r): temperature profile
- a: radiation constant (defines u = aT⁴)
- c: speed of light
- κ: opacity (photon interaction strength per unit mass)
- ρ: density
- L/(4πr²): outward energy flux
- Negative sign: temperature decreases outward when energy flows outward

## 2) Fusion energy: binding energy and Q-value

### 2.1 Nuclear binding energy and mass defect
**Result**
$$
m(A,Z)=Zm_p+(A-Z)m_n-B(A,Z)
$$

**Derivation**
1) A nucleus has Z protons and (A − Z) neutrons.  
2) If unbound, total mass would be:
$$
m_{\mathrm{free}}=Zm_p+(A-Z)m_n
$$
3) Binding energy B is released when nucleons bind, so the bound nucleus has less mass:
$$
m(A,Z)=m_{\mathrm{free}}-B(A,Z)
$$
4) Substitute:
$$
m(A,Z)=Zm_p+(A-Z)m_n-B(A,Z)
$$

**Meaning of terms**
- m(A,Z): nuclear mass
- m_p, m_n: proton and neutron masses
- B(A,Z): binding energy (energy needed to break the nucleus apart)

---

### 2.2 Net reaction for converting hydrogen into helium (pp-chain net)
**Net reaction**
$$
4p+2e^- \rightarrow {}^{4}\mathrm{He}+2\nu_e+Q
$$

**Q-value definition**
$$
Q = m_{\mathrm{initial}}-m_{\mathrm{final}}
$$

**Derivation of Q in terms of particle masses**
1) Initial rest mass:
$$
m_{\mathrm{initial}}=4m_p+2m_e
$$
2) Final rest mass is dominated by helium (neutrino rest masses negligible on MeV scales):
$$
m_{\mathrm{final}}\approx m_{\mathrm{He}}
$$
3) Therefore:
$$
Q = 4m_p+2m_e-m_{\mathrm{He}}
$$

**Rewrite Q using helium binding energy**
1) For helium-4:
$$
m_{\mathrm{He}}=2m_p+2m_n-B(4,2)
$$
2) Substitute into Q:
$$
Q = 4m_p+2m_e-\left(2m_p+2m_n-B(4,2)\right)
$$
3) Simplify:
$$
Q = 2m_p+2m_e-2m_n+B(4,2)
$$
4) Rearrange to highlight neutron–proton conversion:
$$
Q = B(4,2)+2m_e-2(m_n-m_p)
$$

**Physical meaning**
- The large helium binding energy drives energy release.
- Two protons must convert into two neutrons (weak interaction), producing two electron neutrinos.

## 3) Why fusion is difficult: Coulomb barrier and tunneling

### 3.1 Coulomb barrier
**Potential energy**
$$
U(r)=\frac{Z_1Z_2e^2}{r}
$$

**Meaning**
- Z₁e and Z₂e are the charges of the two nuclei.
- To fuse, they must approach to nuclear distances (around femtometres), where U is very large.

**Order-of-magnitude at nuclear scale**
Using the common nuclear-physics conversion:
$$
\frac{e^2}{4\pi\epsilon_0}\approx 1.44\ \mathrm{MeV\ fm}
$$
For proton–proton at r = 1 fm:
$$
U \approx 1.44\ \mathrm{MeV}\approx 10^6\ \mathrm{eV}
$$
Convert energy to temperature using:
$$
E=k_BT
$$
and
$$
1\ \mathrm{eV}\approx 1.16\times 10^4\ \mathrm{K}
$$
so:
$$
10^6\ \mathrm{eV}\approx 1.16\times 10^{10}\ \mathrm{K}
$$

**Key takeaway**
- Classical “over the barrier” fusion would require temperatures around 10¹⁰ K, far hotter than the Sun.

---

### 3.2 Reaction rate for two species in a plasma
**Result**
$$
R_{A+B}=\frac{N_A N_B}{1+\delta_{AB}}\langle\sigma v\rangle
$$

**Derivation**
1) For one particle of type A moving through targets B, interaction rate is:
$$
\text{rate per A} = N_B\,\sigma\,v
$$
2) Multiply by density of A:
$$
R_{A+B}=N_A N_B\,\sigma\,v
$$
3) Thermal averaging over speeds gives:
$$
R_{A+B}=N_A N_B\,\langle\sigma v\rangle
$$
4) If A = B, pair counting double-counts; correct with 1/2. Use:
$$
\frac{1}{1+\delta_{AB}}=
\begin{cases}
1 & A\neq B\\
\frac{1}{2} & A=B
\end{cases}
$$
5) Final:
$$
R_{A+B}=\frac{N_A N_B}{1+\delta_{AB}}\langle\sigma v\rangle
$$

**Meaning of terms**
- R_{A+B}: reactions per unit volume per unit time
- N_A, N_B: number densities
- σ: cross section
- v: relative speed
- ⟨σv⟩: thermal average over the velocity distribution
- δ_AB: Kronecker delta for identical-particle correction

---

### 3.3 Thermal population (Maxwell–Boltzmann tail)
**Result**
$$
N(E)\propto e^{-E/(k_BT)}
$$

**Derivation**
In thermal equilibrium, probability to have energy E is proportional to the Boltzmann factor:
$$
P(E)\propto e^{-E/(k_BT)}
$$
Number of particles around energy E follows the same exponential dependence:
$$
N(E)\propto e^{-E/(k_BT)}
$$

---

### 3.4 Quantum tunneling probability (Gamow factor)
**Result form**
$$
P_t(E)\propto e^{-b/\sqrt{E}}
$$

**Derivation (WKB outline)**
1) Coulomb barrier:
$$
V(r)=\frac{Z_1Z_2e^2}{r}
$$
2) Turning point where V equals kinetic energy E:
$$
V(R_C)=E
$$
so:
$$
R_C=\frac{Z_1Z_2e^2}{E}
$$
3) WKB tunneling probability through the classically forbidden region:
$$
P_t \propto \exp\left(-2\int_{R_N}^{R_C}\sqrt{\frac{2\mu}{\hbar^2}\left(V(r)-E\right)}\,dr\right)
$$
4) Evaluating the integral gives the standard Coulomb tunneling dependence:
$$
P_t \propto e^{-2\pi\eta}
$$
where:
$$
\eta=\frac{Z_1Z_2e^2}{\hbar v}
$$
5) Relate speed to energy:
$$
E=\frac{1}{2}\mu v^2
$$
so:
$$
v\propto \sqrt{E}
$$
Thus:
$$
\eta\propto \frac{1}{\sqrt{E}}
$$
6) Therefore:
$$
P_t(E)\propto e^{-b/\sqrt{E}}
$$
where b is a constant containing Z₁, Z₂, μ, e, and ħ.

---

### 3.5 Why fusion rates are extremely temperature sensitive (Gamow peak idea)
The energy-dependent part of the rate is roughly the product of:
- thermal population
- tunneling probability

So an approximate integrand behaves like:
$$
\text{integrand}(E)\propto e^{-E/(k_BT)}\,e^{-b/\sqrt{E}}
$$
This product peaks at a characteristic energy (Gamow peak). Small changes in T shift the peak and strongly change the rate.

## 4) Neutrino detection: probabilities and event counts

### 4.1 Interaction probability through a detector (thin target)
**Result**
$$
P \approx n_t\sigma L
$$

**Derivation**
1) Probability of interaction in a thin slice dx:
$$
dP = n_t\sigma\,dx
$$
2) Exact “no interaction” survival probability over length L:
$$
P_{\mathrm{no\ int}}=\exp(-n_t\sigma L)
$$
3) Interaction probability:
$$
P_{\mathrm{int}}=1-\exp(-n_t\sigma L)
$$
4) For neutrinos, the optical depth is tiny:
$$
n_t\sigma L\ll 1
$$
so:
$$
\exp(-n_t\sigma L)\approx 1-n_t\sigma L
$$
5) Therefore:
$$
P_{\mathrm{int}}\approx n_t\sigma L
$$

**Meaning of terms**
- n_t: target number density in detector
- σ: interaction cross section
- L: path length through detector

---

### 4.2 Expected number of detected events
**Result (two equivalent forms)**
$$
N = F\,A\,P\,T
$$
and
$$
N = F\,V\,n_t\,\sigma\,T
$$

**Derivation**
1) Flux definition:
$$
F=\frac{\text{neutrinos crossing}}{\text{area}\times \text{time}}
$$
2) Number crossing area A in time T:
$$
N_{\mathrm{cross}}=FAT
$$
3) Expected interactions:
$$
N = N_{\mathrm{cross}}\,P = FAT\,P
$$
4) Use thin-target probability:
$$
P\approx n_t\sigma L
$$
so:
$$
N\approx FAT\,n_t\sigma L
$$
5) Identify volume:
$$
V=AL
$$
Thus:
$$
N\approx F\,V\,n_t\,\sigma\,T
$$

**Meaning of terms**
- N: expected detected interactions
- F: neutrino flux
- A: effective detector area
- V: effective detector volume
- T: exposure time
- n_t: target density
- σ: cross section

## 5) Classic solar neutrino experiments and units

### 5.1 Radiochemical: Homestake (chlorine)
**Reaction**
$$
\nu_e + {}^{37}_{17}\mathrm{Cl}\rightarrow {}^{37}_{18}\mathrm{Ar}+e^-
$$
Idea: count produced argon atoms after exposure (radiochemical extraction).

---

### 5.2 Solar Neutrino Unit (SNU)
**Definition**
$$
1\ \mathrm{SNU}=10^{-36}\ \mathrm{interactions\ atom^{-1}\ s^{-1}}
$$

**Why define this**
Capture rates per atom are extremely small (around 10⁻³⁶ per second), so SNU makes numbers convenient.

---

### 5.3 Radiochemical: Gallium
**Reaction**
$$
\nu_e + {}^{71}\mathrm{Ga}\rightarrow {}^{71}\mathrm{Ge}+e^-
$$
Gallium has a lower threshold than chlorine, so it is sensitive to lower-energy solar neutrinos (e.g., pp neutrinos).

---

### 5.4 Water Cherenkov: Kamiokande / Super-K
**Elastic scattering**
$$
\nu_e+e^- \rightarrow \nu_e+e^-
$$

**Cherenkov threshold (detector threshold)**
Cherenkov emission requires:
$$
v>\frac{c}{n}
$$
Define:
$$
\beta=\frac{v}{c}
$$
Then:
$$
\beta>\frac{1}{n}
$$
Relate to Lorentz factor:
$$
\gamma=\frac{1}{\sqrt{1-\beta^2}}
$$
Electron total energy:
$$
E_e=\gamma m_ec^2
$$
At threshold:
$$
\gamma_{\mathrm{thr}}=\frac{1}{\sqrt{1-\frac{1}{n^2}}}
$$
So:
$$
E_{e,\mathrm{thr}}=\frac{m_ec^2}{\sqrt{1-\frac{1}{n^2}}}
$$

## 6) Solar neutrino problem and survival probability

### 6.1 Definition of survival probability
Operationally, compare observed electron-neutrino flux to Standard Solar Model prediction:
$$
P_{ee}=\frac{\Phi_e^{\mathrm{obs}}}{\Phi_e^{\mathrm{SSM}}}
$$

**Derivation**
Assume flavor change reduces the electron-neutrino component by a factor P_{ee}:
$$
\Phi_e^{\mathrm{obs}} = P_{ee}\,\Phi_e^{\mathrm{SSM}}
$$
Rearrange:
$$
P_{ee}=\frac{\Phi_e^{\mathrm{obs}}}{\Phi_e^{\mathrm{SSM}}}
$$

---

### 6.2 Vacuum oscillation survival probability (two-flavor)
**Mixing**
$$
|\nu_e\rangle=\cos\theta\,|\nu_1\rangle+\sin\theta\,|\nu_2\rangle
$$

**Propagation phases**
$$
|\nu_i(L)\rangle=e^{-iE_iL}\,|\nu_i\rangle
$$

**Amplitude to remain electron flavor**
$$
A_{ee}=\langle \nu_e|\nu_e(L)\rangle=\cos^2\theta\,e^{-iE_1L}+\sin^2\theta\,e^{-iE_2L}
$$

**Probability**
$$
P_{ee}=|A_{ee}|^2
$$

**Ultrarelativistic phase difference**
Using:
$$
E_i\simeq p+\frac{m_i^2}{2E}
$$
so:
$$
(E_2-E_1)L\simeq \frac{\Delta m^2\,L}{2E}
$$
with:
$$
\Delta m^2 = m_2^2-m_1^2
$$

**Final vacuum survival probability**
$$
P_{ee}=1-\sin^2(2\theta)\,\sin^2\left(\frac{\Delta m^2\,L}{4E}\right)
$$

**Meaning of terms**
- θ: mixing angle (oscillation amplitude)
- Δm²: mass-squared splitting (oscillation frequency)
- L: baseline (Sun–Earth distance)
- E: neutrino energy (creates energy dependence)

---

### 6.3 Why the suppression is energy dependent (qualitative)
- Low-energy solar neutrinos tend to follow the vacuum-oscillation behaviour.
- Higher-energy neutrinos are strongly affected by matter in the Sun (MSW effect), leading to a different effective mixing and a different P_{ee} at high energy.
