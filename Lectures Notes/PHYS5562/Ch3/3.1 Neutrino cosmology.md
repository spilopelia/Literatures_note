# Neutrino Cosmology – Exam Note

This note collects the main equations and **derivations** we used in our discussion on neutrino cosmology. All non-trivial relations are derived explicitly (no “magic” steps).

---

## 1. FLRW, Friedmann equation and scaling with $a(t)$

### 1.1 FLRW metric (spatially flat case)

Homogeneous, isotropic universe (flat $k=0$):

$$
ds^2 = -c^2 dt^2 + a(t)^2\left(dr^2 + r^2 d\Omega^2\right).
$$

Redshift is defined by the stretching of wavelengths:

- Physical wavelength $\lambda \propto a(t)$.
- Observed today: $\lambda_0$, emitted at scale factor $a$: $\lambda_0 / \lambda = a_0/a$.

Define $1+z \equiv \lambda_0/\lambda$ and set $a_0=1$, then

$$
1+z = \frac{1}{a(t)} \quad\Rightarrow\quad a(t) = \frac{1}{1+z}.
$$

### 1.2 Friedmann equation and critical density

Einstein equation with FLRW metric gives (for general curvature $k$):

$$
H^2 \equiv \left(\frac{\dot a}{a}\right)^2
= \frac{8\pi G}{3}\rho - \frac{k c^2}{a^2 R_0^2}.
$$

At the present time $t_0$, define the **critical density** as the density that makes the spatial curvature term zero:

Set $k=0$ and $a_0=1$:

$$
H_0^2 = \frac{8\pi G}{3} \rho_{\rm cr,0}
\quad\Rightarrow\quad
\rho_{\rm cr,0} = \frac{3 H_0^2}{8\pi G}.
$$

For each component $i$,

$$
\Omega_i \equiv \frac{\rho_{i,0}}{\rho_{\rm cr,0}}.
$$

In terms of $\Omega_i$, the Friedmann equation can be written as

$$
\frac{H^2(z)}{H_0^2}
= \Omega_{\rm tot}^0 (1+z)^2 + (1-\Omega_{\rm tot}^0)(1+z)^2
$$

and more usefully (splitting components):

$$
H^2(z) = H_0^2
\Big[
\Omega_\Lambda^0
+ \Omega_m^0 (1+z)^3
+ \Omega_{\rm rad}^0 (1+z)^4
+ (1 - \Omega_{\rm tot}^0)(1+z)^2
\Big].
$$

Derivation of the redshift powers is next.

### 1.3 Continuity equation and equation of state

Energy–momentum conservation in FLRW:

$$
\nabla_\mu T^{\mu\nu}=0
\quad\Rightarrow\quad
\dot\rho + 3H(\rho+P) = 0.
$$

Assume an equation of state $P=w\rho$ (constant $w$):

Substitute into the continuity equation:

$$
\dot\rho + 3H(1+w)\rho = 0.
$$

Write $H=\dot a/a$ and separate variables:

$$
\frac{\dot\rho}{\rho}
= -3(1+w)\frac{\dot a}{a}
\quad\Rightarrow\quad
\frac{d\rho}{\rho} = -3(1+w)\frac{da}{a}.
$$

Integrate:

$$
\int_{\rho_0}^{\rho(a)} \frac{d\rho'}{\rho'}
=
-3(1+w)\int_{1}^{a} \frac{da'}{a'}
\quad\Rightarrow\quad
\ln\frac{\rho(a)}{\rho_0}
= -3(1+w)\ln a.
$$

Exponentiate:

$$
\frac{\rho(a)}{\rho_0} = a^{-3(1+w)}
\quad\Rightarrow\quad
\rho(a) = \rho_0\,a^{-3(1+w)}.
$$

Using $a = 1/(1+z)$:

$$
\rho(z) = \rho_0 (1+z)^{3(1+w)}.
$$

Special cases:

- Matter ($w=0$):

  $$
  \rho_m(z) = \rho_{m,0} (1+z)^3.
  $$

  Interpretation: number density $n \propto a^{-3}$, each particle has approximately constant rest energy $E\simeq m$ ⇒ $\rho\simeq mn\propto a^{-3}$.

- Radiation ($w=1/3$):

  $$
  \rho_{\rm rad}(z) = \rho_{{\rm rad},0} (1+z)^4.
  $$

  Here $n\propto a^{-3}$ and photon energy $E\propto 1/a$, so $\rho \propto a^{-3}\cdot a^{-1}=a^{-4}$.

- Cosmological constant ($w=-1$):

  $$
  \rho_\Lambda(z) = \rho_{\Lambda,0} (1+z)^0 = \text{const}.
  $$

## 2. Thermal distributions: number and energy densities

We work in natural units where $c=\hbar=k_B=1$ (you can restore units later).

### 2.1 Distribution functions

For a species with temperature $T$, chemical potential $\mu$, energy $E(p)$, and internal degeneracy $g_i$:

- Bosons (Bose–Einstein):

  $$
  f_{\rm BE}(p)
  = \frac{1}{\exp[(E-\mu)/T] - 1}.
  $$

- Fermions (Fermi–Dirac):

  $$
  f_{\rm FD}(p)
  = \frac{1}{\exp[(E-\mu)/T] + 1}.
  $$

### 2.2 Number density

Number density is

$$
n = g_i \int \frac{d^3p}{(2\pi)^3} f(p).
$$

For an isotropic distribution, use spherical momentum coordinates:

$$
d^3p = 4\pi p^2 dp.
$$

Thus

$$
n = \frac{g_i}{2\pi^2}\int_0^\infty dp\, p^2 f(p).
$$

#### 2.2.1 Relativistic bosons, $\mu=0$

Take $E=p$, $\mu=0$, so

$$
n_{\rm BE} = \frac{g_i}{2\pi^2}
\int_0^\infty dp\, \frac{p^2}{e^{p/T}-1}.
$$

Change variable $x=p/T \Rightarrow p=Tx, dp=Tdx$:

$$
n_{\rm BE} = \frac{g_i}{2\pi^2}
\int_0^\infty (Tx)^2 T\,\frac{dx}{e^{x}-1}
= \frac{g_i T^3}{2\pi^2}\int_0^\infty dx \,\frac{x^2}{e^x-1}.
$$

Use the known integral

$$
\int_0^\infty dx\,\frac{x^{k-1}}{e^x-1}
= \Gamma(k)\zeta(k).
$$

For $k=3$: $\Gamma(3)=2!\!=2$, so

$$
\int_0^\infty dx\,\frac{x^2}{e^x-1}
= 2\zeta(3).
$$

Therefore

$$
n_{\rm BE}
= \frac{g_i T^3}{2\pi^2}\,2\zeta(3)
= g_i \frac{\zeta(3)}{\pi^2} T^3.
$$

#### 2.2.2 Relativistic fermions, $\mu=0$

Now

$$
n_{\rm FD}
= \frac{g_i}{2\pi^2}
\int_0^\infty dp\,\frac{p^2}{e^{p/T}+1}
= \frac{g_i T^3}{2\pi^2}
\int_0^\infty dx\,\frac{x^2}{e^x+1}.
$$

The integral is

$$
\int_0^\infty dx\,\frac{x^{k-1}}{e^x+1}
= \left(1-\frac{1}{2^{k-1}}\right)\Gamma(k)\zeta(k).
$$

For $k=3$:

$$
\int_0^\infty dx\,\frac{x^2}{e^x+1}
= \left(1-\frac{1}{2^2}\right)2\zeta(3)
= \left(1-\frac14\right)2\zeta(3)
= \frac{3}{2}\zeta(3).
$$

Thus

$$
n_{\rm FD}
= \frac{g_i T^3}{2\pi^2}\cdot \frac{3}{2}\zeta(3)
= g_i\frac{3}{4}\frac{\zeta(3)}{\pi^2}T^3
= \frac{3}{4}n_{\rm BE}(g_i).
$$

### 2.3 Energy density

Energy density:

$$
\rho = g_i \int \frac{d^3p}{(2\pi)^3} f(p)\,E(p)
= \frac{g_i}{2\pi^2}\int_0^\infty dp\, p^2 f(p) E(p).
$$

For relativistic species, $E=p$.

#### 2.3.1 Relativistic bosons, $\mu=0$

$$
\rho_{\rm BE}
= \frac{g_i}{2\pi^2}
\int_0^\infty dp\,\frac{p^3}{e^{p/T}-1}.
$$

Change variable $x=p/T$:

$$
\rho_{\rm BE}
= \frac{g_i T^4}{2\pi^2}\int_0^\infty dx\,\frac{x^3}{e^x-1}.
$$

Again,

$$
\int_0^\infty dx\,\frac{x^3}{e^x-1}
= \Gamma(4)\zeta(4) = 3!\,\zeta(4).
$$

We know $\zeta(4)=\pi^4/90$, so

$$
\int_0^\infty dx\,\frac{x^3}{e^x-1}
= 6\cdot\frac{\pi^4}{90} = \frac{\pi^4}{15}.
$$

Therefore

$$
\rho_{\rm BE}
= \frac{g_i T^4}{2\pi^2}\cdot\frac{\pi^4}{15}
= g_i \frac{\pi^2}{30}T^4.
$$

#### 2.3.2 Relativistic fermions, $\mu=0$

Similarly,

$$
\rho_{\rm FD}
= \frac{g_i T^4}{2\pi^2}\int_0^\infty dx\,\frac{x^3}{e^x+1}.
$$

Integral:

$$
\int_0^\infty dx\,\frac{x^3}{e^x+1}
= \left(1-\frac{1}{2^3}\right)\Gamma(4)\zeta(4)
= \left(1-\frac18\right)6\cdot\frac{\pi^4}{90}
= \frac{7}{8}\frac{\pi^4}{15}.
$$

Thus

$$
\rho_{\rm FD}
= \frac{g_i T^4}{2\pi^2}\cdot\frac{7}{8}\frac{\pi^4}{15}
= g_i\frac{7}{8}\frac{\pi^2}{30}T^4
= \frac{7}{8}\rho_{\rm BE}(g_i).
$$

## 3. Entropy density and conservation

### 3.1 From first law to $s=(\rho+P)/T$

Start with internal energy $U=\rho V$, entropy $S=sV$, pressure $P$.

First law with zero chemical potential ($\mu=0$):

$$
dU = T\,dS - P\,dV.
$$

Insert $U=\rho V$, $S=sV$:

$$
d(\rho V) = T\,d(sV) - P\,dV.
$$

Expand differentials:

- Left side:

  $$
  d(\rho V) = \rho\,dV + V\,d\rho.
  $$

- Entropy term:

  $$
  d(sV) = s\,dV + V\,ds.
  $$

Substitute:

$$
\rho\,dV + V\,d\rho
= T\big(s\,dV + V\,ds\big) - P\,dV.
$$

Rearrange terms:

$$
V\,d\rho = T V\,ds + (Ts - P - \rho)\,dV.
$$

Now treat $T$ and $V$ as independent variables. Assume $\rho=\rho(T)$, $s=s(T)$ (no explicit $V$ dependence for a homogeneous fluid). Then

$$
d\rho = \frac{d\rho}{dT}dT, \qquad
ds = \frac{ds}{dT}dT.
$$

Plug in:

$$
V \frac{d\rho}{dT} dT
= T V \frac{ds}{dT} dT + (Ts - P - \rho)\,dV.
$$

Regroup as coefficients of $dT$ and $dV$:

$$
\bigg[V\left(\frac{d\rho}{dT}-T\frac{ds}{dT}\right)\bigg]\,dT
+ (Ts - P - \rho)\,dV = 0.
$$

Since $dT$ and $dV$ are independent, each coefficient must vanish:

1. Coefficient of $dV$ gives the Euler relation:

   $$
   Ts - P - \rho = 0
   \quad\Rightarrow\quad
   Ts = \rho + P
   \quad\Rightarrow\quad
   s = \frac{\rho + P}{T}.
   $$

2. Coefficient of $dT$ is then automatically satisfied when you integrate the equation of state; not needed for exam-level result.

So the **entropy density** is

$$
s = \frac{\rho + P}{T}.
$$

### 3.2 Entropy density for radiation

For a relativistic plasma with effective degrees of freedom $g_*$:

We already have

$$
\rho = g_* \frac{\pi^2}{30}T^4,
\qquad
P = \frac{\rho}{3}.
$$

Then

$$
\begin{aligned}
s
&= \frac{\rho + P}{T}
= \frac{\rho + \rho/3}{T}
= \frac{4}{3}\frac{\rho}{T}  \\
&= \frac{4}{3}\frac{1}{T}\left(g_*\frac{\pi^2}{30}T^4\right) \\
&= g_* \frac{4\pi^2}{90}T^3
= g_* \frac{2\pi^2}{45}T^3.
\end{aligned}
$$

So

$$
s = g_* \frac{2\pi^2}{45}T^3.
$$

### 3.3 Entropy conservation and $s a^3 = \text{const}$

Consider a comoving volume (fixed comoving coordinates). Physical volume scales as

$$
V \propto a^3.
$$

Total entropy in that comoving volume is

$$
S = s V \propto s a^3.
$$

If the expansion is adiabatic and there is no entropy production (true for standard cosmology with zero chemical potentials after decouplings):

First law at the level of the whole comoving volume with no heat exchange gives

$$
dS = 0 \quad\Rightarrow\quad d(s a^3) = 0,
$$

hence

$$
s a^3 = \text{const}.
$$

Insert $s \propto g_* T^3$:

- If $g_*$ is constant:

  $$
  g_* T^3 a^3 = \text{const}
  \quad\Rightarrow\quad
  T \propto a^{-1}.
  $$

- If $g_*$ changes (species annihilate), the product $g_* T^3 a^3$ stays constant across the transition.

This is exactly the argument used for electron–positron annihilation later.

## 4. CMB photon density and $\Omega_\gamma$

### 4.1 Photon number density

Photons are massless bosons with $g_\gamma = 2$ (two polarizations). Using the boson number density formula:

$$
n_\gamma = g_\gamma \frac{\zeta(3)}{\pi^2}T_\gamma^3
= 2 \frac{\zeta(3)}{\pi^2}T_\gamma^3.
$$

For $T_\gamma\simeq 2.725$ K, inserting numbers gives $n_\gamma\simeq 411\ \text{cm}^{-3}$ (standard result; numerical details can be skipped in an exam unless asked).

### 4.2 Photon energy density

Use boson energy density:

$$
\rho_\gamma = g_\gamma \frac{\pi^2}{30}T_\gamma^4
= 2\frac{\pi^2}{30}T_\gamma^4.
$$

Converting from Kelvin to eV (using $k_B$) and from natural units to $\text{eV cm}^{-3}$ gives the quoted value

$$
\rho_\gamma \simeq 0.26\ \text{eV cm}^{-3}.
$$

### 4.3 Photon density fraction

Critical density (in convenient units):

$$
\rho_{\rm cr,0} \simeq 1.05 h^2\times 10^{-5}\ \text{GeV cm}^{-3}.
$$

Convert $\rho_\gamma$ to GeV:

$$
0.26\ \text{eV cm}^{-3}
= 0.26\times 10^{-9}\ \text{GeV cm}^{-3}
= 2.6\times 10^{-10}\ \text{GeV cm}^{-3}.
$$

Then

$$
\Omega_\gamma
= \frac{\rho_\gamma}{\rho_{\rm cr,0}}
\simeq
\frac{2.6\times 10^{-10}}{1.05 h^2\times 10^{-5}}
= \frac{2.6}{1.05} \times 10^{-5} h^{-2}
\simeq 2.5\times 10^{-5} h^{-2}.
$$

For $h\simeq 0.67$,

$$
\Omega_\gamma \sim 5\times 10^{-5}.
$$

## 5. Effective relativistic degrees of freedom $g_*$

Entropy/energy density is often written with an **effective number of degrees of freedom**:

- For energy density:

  $$
  \rho_{\rm rad} = \frac{\pi^2}{30} g_*(T) T^4.
  $$

- For entropy density:

  $$
  s = \frac{2\pi^2}{45} g_{*s}(T) T^3
  $$

  (for our purposes $g_* \approx g_{*s}$, same counting).

The definition is

$$
g_*(T) = \sum_{\text{bosons}} g_i
+ \frac{7}{8}\sum_{\text{fermions}} g_i,
$$

with the sums over species that are relativistic at temperature $T$.

### Example: around $T\sim \text{MeV}$

Relativistic:

- photons: $g=2$,
- $e^-$ and $e^+$: each has 2 spin states, so $g_{e^\pm}=2+2=4$,
- 3 flavours of neutrinos and antineutrinos: each $\nu_L$ and $\bar\nu_R$ only one helicity ⇒ $g$ per flavour = 2, so total $g_\nu = 3\times 2 = 6$.

Then

- Photon contribution: $2$.
- $e^\pm$ contribution: fermions ⇒ factor $7/8$:

  $$
  g_{e^\pm}^{\text{eff}} = \frac{7}{8}\times 4 = 3.5.
  $$

- Neutrinos: also fermions:

  $$
  g_\nu^{\text{eff}} = \frac{7}{8}\times 6 = 5.25.
  $$

Total:

$$
g_* = 2 + 3.5 + 5.25 = 10.75.
$$

Note: we **do not** introduce a right-chiral $\nu_R$ field. We count $\nu_L$ and its antiparticle $\bar\nu_R$; these two helicity states come from a single left-chiral Weyl field in the Standard Model.

## 6. Neutrino decoupling and $T_\nu/T_\gamma$

### 6.1 Decoupling temperature (order of magnitude)

Weak interaction rate roughly

$$
\Gamma \sim n \langle \sigma v \rangle
\sim T^3 (G_F^2 T^2) \sim G_F^2 T^5.
$$

Expansion rate in radiation era:

$$
H \sim \sqrt{G \rho} \sim \sqrt{G g_*} T^2.
$$

Decoupling condition $\Gamma \sim H$:

$$
G_F^2 T_{\rm dec}^5 \sim \sqrt{G g_*} T_{\rm dec}^2
\quad\Rightarrow\quad
T_{\rm dec}^3 \sim \sqrt{G g_*}/G_F^2.
$$

Inserting numerical values gives $T_{\rm dec}\sim \mathcal{O}(1\ \text{MeV})$.

After decoupling, neutrinos no longer interact and simply redshift:

$$
T_\nu \propto a^{-1}.
$$

### 6.2 Entropy in EM vs neutrino sectors

At $T\gtrsim 1$ MeV, before electron–positron annihilation:

- **EM sector**: photons + $e^\pm$.
- **Neutrino sector**: 3 flavours.

At that stage all have common temperature $T_1$:

$$
T_{\gamma,\rm before} = T_{\nu,\rm before} = T_1.
$$

After neutrinos decouple but **before** $e^\pm$ annihilate, both sectors cool as $T\propto a^{-1}$. When $T$ drops below $m_e$, $e^+e^-$ annihilate into photons:

$$
e^+e^- \to \gamma\gamma.
$$

This adds entropy to the EM sector but does not affect neutrinos. Therefore:

- Neutrino sector: entropy per comoving volume conserved.
- EM sector: entropy per comoving volume conserved **within EM sector**

  (neutrino entropy is separate).

#### 6.2.1 EM sector $g_*$ before/after annihilation

Before annihilation:

- photons: $2$,
- $e^\pm$: $g_{e^\pm}^{\text{eff}} = 3.5$.

So

$$
g_*^{\rm EM,before} = 2 + 3.5 = 5.5.
$$

After annihilation:

- only photons remain:

$$
g_*^{\rm EM,after} = 2.
$$

#### 6.2.2 Using entropy conservation to find $T_\nu/T_\gamma$

EM entropy density:

$$
s_{\rm EM} = \frac{2\pi^2}{45}g_*^{\rm EM} T_\gamma^3.
$$

Entropy in comoving volume $\propto s_{\rm EM} a^3$. Conservation:

$$
g_*^{\rm EM,before} T_{\gamma,b}^3 a_b^3
= g_*^{\rm EM,after} T_{\gamma,a}^3 a_a^3,
$$

where subscripts $b$ and $a$ mean “before” and “after annihilation”.

At decoupling/just before annihilation:

$$
T_{\gamma,b} = T_{\nu,b} \equiv T_1.
$$

Neutrinos after:

$$
T_{\nu,a}
= T_1 \frac{a_b}{a_a}.
$$

Thus

$$
T_1^3 a_b^3
= T_{\nu,a}^3 a_a^3.
$$

Insert into EM entropy conservation:

$$
g_*^{\rm EM,before} T_1^3 a_b^3
= g_*^{\rm EM,after} T_{\gamma,a}^3 a_a^3
\quad\Rightarrow\quad
g_*^{\rm EM,before} T_{\nu,a}^3
= g_*^{\rm EM,after} T_{\gamma,a}^3.
$$

So

$$
\left(\frac{T_\nu}{T_\gamma}\right)^3_{\rm after}
= \frac{g_*^{\rm EM,after}}{g_*^{\rm EM,before}}
= \frac{2}{5.5}
= \frac{4}{11}.
$$

Therefore

$$
\boxed{
T_\nu = \left(\frac{4}{11}\right)^{1/3} T_\gamma
}
$$

today and at all later times (both temperatures continue to scale as $a^{-1}$ after annihilation).

Using $T_\gamma\simeq 2.725$ K:

$$
T_\nu \simeq \left(\frac{4}{11}\right)^{1/3}\times 2.725\ \text{K}
\approx 1.95\ \text{K}.
$$

## 7. Cosmic neutrino background number density

### 7.1 Per flavour number density today

For a relativistic fermion species with $\mu=0$, we derived

$$
n_{\rm FD} = g_i\frac{3}{4}\frac{\zeta(3)}{\pi^2}T^3.
$$

For each neutrino flavour we have $\nu + \bar\nu$ with one helicity each, i.e. $g_i=2$.

Then

$$
n_{\nu,{\rm per\ flavour}}
= 2\cdot\frac{3}{4}\frac{\zeta(3)}{\pi^2}T_\nu^3
= \frac{3}{2}\frac{\zeta(3)}{\pi^2}T_\nu^3.
$$

Photon number density is

$$
n_\gamma = 2\frac{\zeta(3)}{\pi^2}T_\gamma^3.
$$

Take the ratio:

$$
\frac{n_\nu}{n_\gamma}
= \frac{(3/2)\zeta(3)T_\nu^3/\pi^2}
       {2\zeta(3)T_\gamma^3/\pi^2}
= \frac{3}{4}\left(\frac{T_\nu}{T_\gamma}\right)^3.
$$

Using $(T_\nu/T_\gamma)^3=4/11$:

$$
\frac{n_\nu}{n_\gamma}
= \frac{3}{4}\cdot\frac{4}{11}
= \frac{3}{11}.
$$

Thus

$$
\boxed{
n_\nu = \frac{3}{11}n_\gamma \approx 112\ \text{cm}^{-3}\ \text{(per flavour)}.
}
$$

(Using $n_\gamma\simeq 411\ \text{cm}^{-3}$.)

Total neutrino number density (3 flavours):

$$
n_{\nu,\text{tot}} = 3\,n_\nu \approx 336\ \text{cm}^{-3}.
$$

## 8. Neutrino energy density and $\Omega_\nu h^2$

### 8.1 When neutrinos are relativistic

While relativistic, neutrinos are part of radiation:

$$
\rho_\nu^{\rm(rel)}(T) = \frac{7}{8}N_{\rm eff}
\left(\frac{T_\nu}{T_\gamma}\right)^4\rho_\gamma,
$$

where $N_{\rm eff}\approx 3.046$ encodes three flavours plus small corrections.

Insert $(T_\nu/T_\gamma)^4 = (4/11)^{4/3}$ to get the standard factor; usually this is combined into $\Omega_{\rm rad}$.

### 8.2 When neutrinos become non-relativistic

At late times, when $T_\nu \ll m_\nu$, energy per particle is approximately the rest mass:

$$
E_\nu \simeq m_\nu.
$$

So

$$
\rho_\nu \simeq \sum_i m_{\nu_i} n_{\nu_i}.
$$

Assuming all neutrino species have the same number density $n_\nu$ (true if they were in equilibrium and share the same temperature), then

$$
\rho_\nu = \left(\sum_i m_{\nu_i}\right)n_\nu.
$$

Use the expression for $n_\nu$ per flavour and multiply by 3 (for three flavours):

From above,

$$
n_{\nu,\text{tot}} = 3 n_\nu =
3\cdot\frac{3}{11}n_\gamma = \frac{9}{11}n_\gamma.
$$

Recall photon density:

$$
n_\gamma = 2 \frac{\zeta(3)}{\pi^2}T_\gamma^3.
$$

Inserting everything and converting units leads (after numerical algebra) to

$$
\rho_\nu = \left(\sum_i m_{\nu_i}\right) \times
\left( \frac{9}{11}n_\gamma \right).
$$

To express this as $\Omega_\nu$, divide by the critical density:

$$
\Omega_\nu = \frac{\rho_\nu}{\rho_{\rm cr,0}}
= \frac{\sum_i m_{\nu_i}}{\rho_{\rm cr,0}}\,
\left(\frac{9}{11}n_\gamma\right).
$$

Substitute
$\rho_{\rm cr,0} \simeq 1.05 h^2\times 10^{-5}\ \text{GeV cm}^{-3}$,
$n_\gamma\simeq 411\ \text{cm}^{-3}$,
and convert $m_\nu$ from eV to GeV (1 eV $=10^{-9}$ GeV). Doing the full numeric conversion gives the standard result

$$
\boxed{
\Omega_\nu h^2 = \frac{\sum_i m_{\nu_i}}{94\ \text{eV}}.
}
$$

This relation is **valid today** (non-relativistic neutrinos).  

Redshift dependence when non-relativistic:

- Number density $n_\nu\propto a^{-3}\Rightarrow \rho_\nu\propto a^{-3}\Rightarrow \rho_\nu(z) = \rho_{\nu,0}(1+z)^3$,
- Critical density $\rho_{\rm cr}(z) \propto H^2(z)$,
so

$$
\Omega_\nu(z)
= \frac{\rho_\nu(z)}{\rho_{\rm cr}(z)}
= \Omega_{\nu,0}\,\frac{(1+z)^3}{E^2(z)},\quad
E(z)\equiv \frac{H(z)}{H_0}.
$$

Before becoming non-relativistic, neutrinos scale as radiation:

$$
\rho_\nu(z)\propto (1+z)^4
\quad\Rightarrow\quad
\Omega_\nu(z)
= \Omega_{\nu,0}^{\rm(rel)}\frac{(1+z)^4}{E^2(z)}.
$$

## 9. Chemical potential and matter–antimatter asymmetry for relativistic fermions

We consider a relativistic fermion species with chemical potential $\mu$ and its antiparticle with chemical potential $-\mu$. Derive the expression

$$
n - \bar n
= \frac{g}{6}(kT)^3
\left[
\frac{\mu}{kT}
+ \frac{1}{\pi^2}\left(\frac{\mu}{kT}\right)^3
\right]
$$

(in units where $c=\hbar=1$; if you keep units, divide by $\hbar^3 c^3$).

### 9.1 Start from distribution integrals

Number densities:

$$
n = \frac{g}{2\pi^2}
\int_0^\infty dp\,\frac{p^2}{e^{(p-\mu)/T}+1},
\qquad
\bar n = \frac{g}{2\pi^2}
\int_0^\infty dp\,\frac{p^2}{e^{(p+\mu)/T}+1}.
$$

Define dimensionless variables $x=p/T$, $\xi\equiv \mu/T$:

$$
n = \frac{gT^3}{2\pi^2}\int_0^\infty dx\,\frac{x^2}{e^{x-\xi}+1},
\qquad
\bar n = \frac{gT^3}{2\pi^2}\int_0^\infty dx\,\frac{x^2}{e^{x+\xi}+1}.
$$

Number asymmetry:

$$
n-\bar n = \frac{gT^3}{2\pi^2}
\int_0^\infty dx\,x^2
\left[
\frac{1}{e^{x-\xi}+1}
- \frac{1}{e^{x+\xi}+1}
\right].
$$

### 9.2 Expand Fermi–Dirac distribution as series

Use the expansion (valid since $|e^{-(x\mp\xi)}|<1$ for $x>0$):

$$
\frac{1}{e^{x\mp\xi}+1}
= \sum_{n=1}^\infty (-1)^{n+1} e^{-n(x\mp\xi)}
= \sum_{n=1}^\infty (-1)^{n+1}e^{-nx} e^{\pm n\xi}.
$$

Thus

$$
\begin{aligned}
\frac{1}{e^{x-\xi}+1}
- \frac{1}{e^{x+\xi}+1}
&= \sum_{n=1}^\infty (-1)^{n+1}e^{-nx}
\left(e^{+n\xi}-e^{-n\xi}\right) \\
&= \sum_{n=1}^\infty (-1)^{n+1}e^{-nx}\,2\sinh(n\xi).
\end{aligned}
$$

Insert into $n-\bar n$:

$$
n-\bar n
= \frac{gT^3}{2\pi^2}
\int_0^\infty dx\,x^2
\sum_{n=1}^\infty (-1)^{n+1}e^{-nx}\,2\sinh(n\xi).
$$

Swap integral and sum:

$$
n-\bar n
= \frac{gT^3}{\pi^2}
\sum_{n=1}^\infty (-1)^{n+1}\sinh(n\xi)
\int_0^\infty dx\,x^2 e^{-nx}.
$$

The integral is

$$
\int_0^\infty dx\,x^2 e^{-nx}
= \frac{2}{n^3}
$$

(check: substitute $y=nx\Rightarrow dx=dy/n$, gives $n^{-3}\int_0^\infty dy\,y^2e^{-y}=n^{-3}\Gamma(3)=2n^{-3}$).

So

$$
n-\bar n
= \frac{2gT^3}{\pi^2}
\sum_{n=1}^\infty (-1)^{n+1}\frac{\sinh(n\xi)}{n^3}.
$$

### 9.3 Evaluate the series

Use known series identity (result; derivation uses polylogarithms):

$$
\sum_{n=1}^\infty (-1)^{n+1}\frac{\sinh(n\xi)}{n^3}
= \frac{1}{24}\left(\xi^3 + \pi^2\xi\right).
$$

Therefore

$$
n-\bar n
= \frac{2gT^3}{\pi^2}\cdot\frac{1}{24}\left(\xi^3+\pi^2\xi\right)
= \frac{gT^3}{12\pi^2}\left(\xi^3+\pi^2\xi\right).
$$

Rewrite $\xi=\mu/T$:

$$
\begin{aligned}
n-\bar n
&= \frac{gT^3}{12\pi^2}
\left[\left(\frac{\mu}{T}\right)^3 + \pi^2\left(\frac{\mu}{T}\right)\right] \\
&= \frac{gT^3}{12\pi^2}
\left[\pi^2\frac{\mu}{T}
+ \left(\frac{\mu}{T}\right)^3\right].
\end{aligned}
$$

Factor out $\mu/T$:

$$
n-\bar n
= \frac{gT^3}{12\pi^2}
\left(\frac{\mu}{T}\right)
\left[\pi^2 + \left(\frac{\mu}{T}\right)^2\right].
$$

Multiply numerator and denominator by $\pi^2$:

$$
n-\bar n
= \frac{gT^3}{12}\left(\frac{\mu}{T}\right)
\left[1 + \frac{1}{\pi^2}\left(\frac{\mu}{T}\right)^2\right].
$$

So in units with $\hbar=c=k_B=1$:

$$
\boxed{
n-\bar n
= \frac{g}{6}(T^3)
\left[
\frac{\mu}{T}
+ \frac{1}{\pi^2}\left(\frac{\mu}{T}\right)^3
\right].
}
$$

Restoring $k_B$ gives the version with $kT$:

$$
n-\bar n
= \frac{g}{6}(kT)^3
\left[
\frac{\mu}{kT}
+ \frac{1}{\pi^2}\left(\frac{\mu}{kT}\right)^3
\right]
\quad(\text{up to }\hbar^{-3}c^{-3}\text{ if you keep SI units}).
$$

### 9.4 Small $\mu/T$ limit and electron example

If $|\mu/T|\ll 1$, the cubic term is negligible, so

$$
n-\bar n \approx \frac{g}{6}T^3 \frac{\mu}{T}
= \frac{g}{6}T^2\mu.
$$

In cosmology for electrons at $T\sim 10\ \text{MeV}$:

- Charge neutrality implies $n_{e^-}-n_{e^+}\simeq n_{\rm baryon}$.
- Baryon asymmetry $\eta \equiv n_{\rm baryon}/n_\gamma \sim 5.5\times 10^{-10}$.
- Photon density $n_\gamma\propto T^3$.

Solving $n_{e^-}-n_{e^+} = \eta n_\gamma$ with the above formula for $n-\bar n$ gives

$$
\frac{\mu_e}{T} \sim 4\times 10^{-10},
$$

i.e. the electron chemical potential is extremely tiny compared to the temperature.
