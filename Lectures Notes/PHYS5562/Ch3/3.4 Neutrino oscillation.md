
# Neutrino Oscillations, MSW, CP, Mass Ordering, and Baryogenesis — Exam Revision Note

## 1) Flavour vs mass states and what “mixing” means

Neutrinos are produced/detected in flavour states and propagate as mass eigenstates.

Define flavour basis states:  
$$  
|\nu_\alpha\rangle,\quad \alpha\in{e,\mu,\tau}.  
$$

Define mass basis states:  
$$  
|\nu_i\rangle,\quad i\in{1,2,3}.  
$$

Mixing (PMNS) relation:  
$$  
|\nu_\alpha\rangle=\sum_{i=1}^{3}U_{\alpha i}^*,|\nu_i\rangle,  
\qquad  
|\nu_i\rangle=\sum_{\alpha=e,\mu,\tau}U_{\alpha i},|\nu_\alpha\rangle.  
$$

Unitarity:  
$$  
U^\dagger U = I.  
$$

---

## 2) Why the propagation Hamiltonian becomes (E + m^2/(2E))

Start from exact relativistic energy:  
$$  
E=\sqrt{p^2+m^2}.  
$$

Factor out (p):  
$$  
E=p\sqrt{1+\frac{m^2}{p^2}}.  
$$

Binomial expansion rule (general rule used in the slide):  
$$  
(1+x)^{1/2}=1+\frac{x}{2}-\frac{x^2}{8}+\cdots,\quad |x|\ll1.  
$$

Take:  
$$  
x=\frac{m^2}{p^2}.  
$$

Keep leading correction:  
$$  
E\simeq p\left(1+\frac{1}{2}\frac{m^2}{p^2}\right)=p+\frac{m^2}{2p}.  
$$

Ultrarelativistic approximation (p\simeq E) (difference is already order (m^2/E)):  
$$  
E\simeq E+\frac{m^2}{2E}.  
$$

Key point for oscillations: only differences in the small term matter:  
$$  
E_i-E_j \simeq \frac{m_i^2-m_j^2}{2E}=\frac{\Delta m_{ij}^2}{2E}.  
$$

## 3) Two-flavour vacuum oscillation: full derivation

### 3.1 Two-flavour mixing definition

# Use 2 flavours (e,\mu) and 2 masses (1,2). Define:  
$$  
\begin{pmatrix}  
|\nu_e\rangle\  
|\nu_\mu\rangle  
\end{pmatrix}

\begin{pmatrix}  
\cos\theta & \sin\theta\  
-\sin\theta & \cos\theta  
\end{pmatrix}  
\begin{pmatrix}  
|\nu_1\rangle\  
|\nu_2\rangle  
\end{pmatrix}.  
$$

So:  
$$  
|\nu_e\rangle=\cos\theta,|\nu_1\rangle+\sin\theta,|\nu_2\rangle.  
$$

### 3.2 Propagation in the mass basis

Time/space evolution phase:  
$$  
|\nu_i(L)\rangle = e^{-iE_i t},|\nu_i(0)\rangle,\quad t\simeq L.  
$$

Use:  
$$  
E_i\simeq E+\frac{m_i^2}{2E}.  
$$

# Then:  
$$  
|\nu_i(L)\rangle

 e^{-i\left(E+\frac{m_i^2}{2E}\right)L},|\nu_i(0)\rangle

e^{-iEL},e^{-i\frac{m_i^2}{2E}L},|\nu_i(0)\rangle.  
$$

### 3.3 Evolve an initial (\nu_e)

At (L=0):  
$$  
|\nu_e(0)\rangle=\cos\theta,|\nu_1\rangle+\sin\theta,|\nu_2\rangle.  
$$

# At baseline (L):  
$$  
|\nu_e(L)\rangle

\cos\theta,e^{-i\frac{m_1^2}{2E}L}|\nu_1\rangle  
+  
\sin\theta,e^{-i\frac{m_2^2}{2E}L}|\nu_2\rangle  
$$  
(up to a common global factor (e^{-iEL})).

Define:  
$$  
\Delta m^2 \equiv m_2^2-m_1^2,  
\qquad  
\phi \equiv \frac{\Delta m^2,L}{2E}.  
$$

Factor out the common phase (e^{-i\frac{m_1^2}{2E}L}):  
$$  
|\nu_e(L)\rangle  
\propto  
\cos\theta,|\nu_1\rangle+\sin\theta,e^{-i\phi}|\nu_2\rangle.  
$$

### 3.4 “Identity term / global phase” rule (from our discussion)

If:  
$$  
H\rightarrow H+\lambda I,  
$$  
then:  
$$  
e^{-i(H+\lambda I)L}=e^{-i\lambda L},e^{-iHL}.  
$$

The prefactor (e^{-i\lambda L}) is a global phase and cancels in probabilities:  
$$  
P_{\alpha\beta}=|\langle\nu_\beta|e^{-iHL}|\nu_\alpha\rangle|^2.  
$$

This is why only (\Delta m^2) (not absolute masses) controls oscillations.

### 3.5 Survival probability (P_{ee})

Amplitude:  
$$  
A_{ee}(L)\equiv \langle\nu_e|\nu_e(L)\rangle.  
$$

Use:  
$$  
\langle\nu_e|=\cos\theta,\langle\nu_1|+\sin\theta,\langle\nu_2|.  
$$

# Compute:  
$$  
A_{ee}(L)

\cos^2\theta+\sin^2\theta,e^{-i\phi}.  
$$

# Probability:  
$$  
P_{ee}=|A_{ee}|^2

(\cos^2\theta+\sin^2\theta e^{-i\phi})(\cos^2\theta+\sin^2\theta e^{i\phi}).  
$$

# Expand:  
$$  
P_{ee}

\cos^4\theta+\sin^4\theta+2\cos^2\theta\sin^2\theta\cos\phi.  
$$

Use:  
$$  
\cos^4\theta+\sin^4\theta=(\cos^2\theta+\sin^2\theta)^2-2\cos^2\theta\sin^2\theta=1-2\cos^2\theta\sin^2\theta.  
$$

# So:  
$$  
P_{ee}

1-2\cos^2\theta\sin^2\theta(1-\cos\phi).  
$$

Use:  
$$  
1-\cos\phi=2\sin^2\left(\frac{\phi}{2}\right),  
\qquad  
\sin^2(2\theta)=4\cos^2\theta\sin^2\theta.  
$$

# Final result:  
$$  
P_{ee}

1-\sin^2(2\theta),\sin^2\left(\frac{\Delta m^2,L}{4E}\right).  
$$

## 4) Three flavours: parameters and why the CP phase is put in (R_{13})

### 4.1 Degrees of freedom counting (what the slide is doing)

A general (3\times3) unitary matrix has:  
$$  
\dim U(3)=9  
$$  
real parameters.

For Dirac neutrinos, field rephasings remove 5 unphysical phases (3 charged lepton phases + 3 neutrino flavour phases − 1 overall phase):  
$$  
9-5=4.  
$$

So oscillations depend on:  
$$  
3\ \text{mixing angles} + 1\ \text{Dirac CP phase}.  
$$

(If neutrinos are Majorana, 2 extra “Majorana phases” exist but do not affect oscillation probabilities.)

### 4.2 Standard parameterization

Write:  
$$  
U = R_{23}(\theta_{23}),R_{13}(\theta_{13},\delta),R_{12}(\theta_{12}).  
$$

Where:  
$$  
R_{12}=  
\begin{pmatrix}  
c_{12} & s_{12} & 0\  
-s_{12} & c_{12} & 0\  
0 & 0 & 1  
\end{pmatrix},  
\quad  
R_{23}=  
\begin{pmatrix}  
1 & 0 & 0\  
0 & c_{23} & s_{23}\  
0 & -s_{23} & c_{23}  
\end{pmatrix}.  
$$

And:  
$$  
R_{13}=  
\begin{pmatrix}  
c_{13} & 0 & s_{13}e^{-i\delta}\  
0 & 1 & 0\  
-s_{13}e^{i\delta} & 0 & c_{13}  
\end{pmatrix}.  
$$

### 4.3 Why put (\delta) in (R_{13})? (from our conversation)

- There is only one physical Dirac phase, so rephasing freedom lets you move it into one convenient place.
    
- Putting it in (R_{13}) makes the physics transparent: if (\theta_{13}=0), then (s_{13}=0) and (\delta) disappears from (U), meaning CP violation in oscillations vanishes in that limit (correct physical behaviour).
    

This is reflected by the Jarlskog invariant (size of CP violation):  
$$  
J=c_{12}s_{12},c_{23}s_{23},c_{13}^2 s_{13},\sin\delta.  
$$

If:  
$$  
s_{13}=0,  
$$  
then:  
$$  
J=0,  
$$  
so no CP violation in oscillations.

## 5) General 3-flavour vacuum probability and the Re/Im decomposition (full derivation)

### 5.1 Start from the amplitude

Initial flavour state:  
$$  
|\nu_\alpha(0)\rangle=\sum_i U_{\alpha i}^*|\nu_i\rangle.  
$$

Propagation:  
$$  
|\nu_\alpha(L)\rangle=\sum_i U_{\alpha i}^* e^{-i\frac{m_i^2}{2E}L}|\nu_i\rangle  
$$  
(dropping the common (e^{-iEL}) phase using the global-phase rule).

Project onto final flavour (\beta):  
$$  
|\nu_\beta\rangle=\sum_j U_{\beta j}^*|\nu_j\rangle,  
\qquad  
\langle\nu_\beta|=\sum_j U_{\beta j}\langle\nu_j|.  
$$

# Amplitude:  
$$  
A_{\alpha\beta}=\langle\nu_\beta|\nu_\alpha(L)\rangle

\sum_{i,j} U_{\beta j},U_{\alpha i}^*,e^{-i\frac{m_i^2}{2E}L}\langle\nu_j|\nu_i\rangle.  
$$

Use orthonormality:  
$$  
\langle\nu_j|\nu_i\rangle=\delta_{ji}.  
$$

So:  
$$  
A_{\alpha\beta}=\sum_i U_{\beta i}U_{\alpha i}^* e^{-i\frac{m_i^2}{2E}L}.  
$$

### 5.2 Square it

# $$  
P_{\alpha\beta}=|A_{\alpha\beta}|^2

\left(\sum_i U_{\beta i}U_{\alpha i}^* e^{-i\phi_i}\right)  
\left(\sum_j U_{\beta j}^*U_{\alpha j} e^{+i\phi_j}\right),  
$$  
with:  
$$  
\phi_i\equiv \frac{m_i^2 L}{2E}.  
$$

# Expand:  
$$  
P_{\alpha\beta}

\sum_{i,j}  
U_{\beta i}U_{\alpha i}^*U_{\beta j}^*U_{\alpha j},e^{-i(\phi_i-\phi_j)}.  
$$

Define:  
$$  
\Delta m_{ij}^2\equiv m_i^2-m_j^2,  
\qquad  
\phi_i-\phi_j=\frac{\Delta m_{ij}^2 L}{2E}.  
$$

Define the quartet:  
$$  
Q_{\alpha\beta}^{ij}\equiv U_{\alpha i}U_{\beta i}^*U_{\alpha j}^*U_{\beta j}.  
$$

# Then the probability can be written (same content, rearranged):  
$$  
P_{\alpha\beta}

\sum_{i,j}  
Q_{\alpha\beta}^{ij},e^{-i\frac{\Delta m_{ij}^2L}{2E}}.  
$$

### 5.3 Pair (ij) with (ji)

Note:  
$$  
Q_{\alpha\beta}^{ji}=(Q_{\alpha\beta}^{ij})^*,  
\qquad  
\Delta m_{ji}^2=-\Delta m_{ij}^2.  
$$

# So the pair contributes:  
$$  
Q^{ij}e^{-i\Delta}+ (Q^{ij})^*e^{+i\Delta}

2,\mathrm{Re}\left(Q^{ij}e^{-i\Delta}\right),  
$$  
with:  
$$  
\Delta\equiv \frac{\Delta m_{ij}^2L}{2E}.  
$$

Write:  
$$  
Q^{ij}=\mathrm{Re}(Q^{ij})+i,\mathrm{Im}(Q^{ij}).  
$$

# Then:  
$$  
\mathrm{Re}\left(Q^{ij}e^{-i\Delta}\right)

\mathrm{Re}(Q^{ij})\cos\Delta+\mathrm{Im}(Q^{ij})\sin\Delta.  
$$

Use:  
$$  
\cos\Delta = 1-2\sin^2\left(\frac{\Delta}{2}\right),  
\qquad  
\sin\Delta = \sin(2X),  
$$  
where:  
$$  
X\equiv \frac{\Delta}{2}=\frac{\Delta m_{ij}^2L}{4E}.  
$$

# After collecting terms and using unitarity to produce the (\delta_{\alpha\beta}) piece, the final standard form is:  
$$  
P_{\alpha\beta}

\delta_{\alpha\beta}  
-4\sum_{i<j}\mathrm{Re}\left(Q_{\alpha\beta}^{ij}\right)\sin^2 X_{ij}  
+2\sum_{i<j}\mathrm{Im}\left(Q_{\alpha\beta}^{ij}\right)\sin 2X_{ij}.  
$$

with:  
$$  
X_{ij}=\frac{\Delta m_{ij}^2L}{4E}.  
$$

## 6) CP in oscillations: what changes for antineutrinos

In vacuum, antineutrino mixing is complex conjugated:  
$$  
U \rightarrow U^*.  
$$

So:  
$$  
\mathrm{Re}(Q_{\alpha\beta}^{ij})\ \text{is unchanged},  
\qquad  
\mathrm{Im}(Q_{\alpha\beta}^{ij})\ \text{changes sign}.  
$$

Therefore:

- The term with (\sin^2 X_{ij}) is CP-even.
    
- The term with (\sin 2X_{ij}) is CP-odd.
    

So CP violation in oscillations is diagnosed by:  
$$  
P(\nu_\alpha\to\nu_\beta)\neq P(\bar\nu_\alpha\to\bar\nu_\beta),  
$$  
which requires:  
$$  
\mathrm{Im}(Q_{\alpha\beta}^{ij})\neq 0,  
$$  
i.e. a genuinely complex PMNS matrix.

## 7) Matter effect (MSW): Hamiltonian, meaning of each term, and (\theta_m)

### 7.1 Flavour-basis evolution equation (2 flavours)

# Write the Schrödinger equation in flavour basis:  
$$  
i\frac{d}{dt}  
\begin{pmatrix}  
|\nu_e\rangle\  
|\nu_\mu\rangle  
\end{pmatrix}

H_f  
\begin{pmatrix}  
|\nu_e\rangle\  
|\nu_\mu\rangle  
\end{pmatrix}.  
$$

# Vacuum part (written in flavour basis):  
$$  
H_{\text{vac}}

\frac{\Delta m^2}{4E}  
\begin{pmatrix}  
-\cos 2\theta & \sin 2\theta\  
\sin 2\theta & \cos 2\theta  
\end{pmatrix}.  
$$

# Matter potential part (ordinary matter has electrons, no muons/taus):  
$$  
H_{\text{mat}}

\begin{pmatrix}  
V & 0\  
0 & 0  
\end{pmatrix},  
\qquad  
V=\sqrt{2}G_F N_e.  
$$

Total:  
$$  
H_f = H_{\text{vac}}+H_{\text{mat}}.  
$$

### 7.2 Rewrite using (A)

Define:  
$$  
A\equiv 2EV=2E\sqrt{2}G_FN_e.  
$$

Then:  
$$  
H_{\text{mat}}=\frac{1}{4E}  
\begin{pmatrix}  
2A & 0\  
0 & 0  
\end{pmatrix}.  
$$

So:  
$$  
H_f=  
\frac{1}{4E}  
\begin{pmatrix}  
-\Delta m^2\cos 2\theta + 2A & \Delta m^2\sin 2\theta\  
\Delta m^2\sin 2\theta & \Delta m^2\cos 2\theta  
\end{pmatrix}.  
$$

Meaning of terms:

- (\Delta m^2/(4E)) sets the vacuum oscillation scale.
    
- (\sin 2\theta) terms are flavour-mixing (off-diagonal).
    
- (\cos 2\theta) terms are relative diagonal splitting in flavour basis.
    
- (A) shifts only the (\nu_e) diagonal term, modifying effective mixing.
    

### 7.3 Does this equation “give” (\theta_m)? (answer from our discussion)

This equation defines the Hamiltonian. The matter mixing angle (\theta_m) is obtained by diagonalising the (2\times2) matrix.

General diagonalisation rule:  
$$  
M=  
\begin{pmatrix}  
a & b\  
b & d  
\end{pmatrix}  
\quad\Rightarrow\quad  
\tan(2\theta_m)=\frac{2b}{d-a}.  
$$

Apply to the matrix inside (H_f) (ignore overall (1/(4E))):  
$$  
a=-\Delta m^2\cos 2\theta+2A,  
\qquad  
b=\Delta m^2\sin 2\theta,  
\qquad  
d=\Delta m^2\cos 2\theta.  
$$

# Compute:  
$$  
d-a

 \Delta m^2\cos 2\theta-(-\Delta m^2\cos 2\theta+2A)

2(\Delta m^2\cos 2\theta-A).  
$$

# Then:  
$$  
\tan(2\theta_m)=\frac{2\Delta m^2\sin 2\theta}{2(\Delta m^2\cos 2\theta-A)}

\frac{\Delta m^2\sin 2\theta}{\Delta m^2\cos 2\theta-A}.  
$$

# Matter-modified splitting:  
$$  
\Delta m_m^2

\sqrt{(\Delta m^2\cos 2\theta-A)^2+(\Delta m^2\sin 2\theta)^2}.  
$$

Resonance (maximal mixing, (\theta_m=\pi/4)):  
$$  
\Delta m^2\cos 2\theta - A = 0  
\quad\Rightarrow\quad  
A=\Delta m^2\cos 2\theta.  
$$

## 8) Varying density and the “extra term” (-iU^\dagger dU/dx) (full derivation from our conversation)

This is the derivation that produces the off-diagonal (\pm i,d\theta_m/dx) terms in the instantaneous matter basis.

### 8.1 Start in flavour basis

Use space evolution (set (t\simeq x) for ultrarelativistic propagation):  
$$  
i\frac{d}{dx}|\nu_f(x)\rangle = H_f(x),|\nu_f(x)\rangle.  
$$

### 8.2 Define instantaneous matter basis by a position-dependent rotation

Let (U_m(x)) diagonalise (H_f(x)):  
$$  
U_m^\dagger(x),H_f(x),U_m(x)=H_m^{\text{diag}}(x).  
$$

Define matter-basis state:  
$$  
|\nu_m(x)\rangle \equiv U_m^\dagger(x),|\nu_f(x)\rangle.  
$$

Invert:  
$$  
|\nu_f(x)\rangle = U_m(x),|\nu_m(x)\rangle.  
$$

### 8.3 Differentiate and substitute

# Differentiate:  
$$  
\frac{d}{dx}|\nu_f\rangle

\frac{dU_m}{dx}|\nu_m\rangle + U_m\frac{d}{dx}|\nu_m\rangle.  
$$

# Plug into flavour Schrödinger equation:  
$$  
i\left(\frac{dU_m}{dx}|\nu_m\rangle + U_m\frac{d}{dx}|\nu_m\rangle\right)

H_f U_m|\nu_m\rangle.  
$$

# Left-multiply by (U_m^\dagger):  
$$  
i\left(U_m^\dagger\frac{dU_m}{dx}|\nu_m\rangle + \frac{d}{dx}|\nu_m\rangle\right)

U_m^\dagger H_f U_m|\nu_m\rangle.  
$$

# Rearrange:  
$$  
i\frac{d}{dx}|\nu_m\rangle

\left(U_m^\dagger H_f U_m - iU_m^\dagger\frac{dU_m}{dx}\right)|\nu_m\rangle.  
$$

**General rule (the one you asked for):** when you change to an (x)-dependent basis, the effective Hamiltonian becomes:  
$$  
H_{\text{eff}}(x)=U^\dagger H U - iU^\dagger\frac{dU}{dx}.  
$$

### 8.4 Evaluate the extra term for 2 flavours

For 2 flavours, take:  
$$  
U_m(x)=  
\begin{pmatrix}  
\cos\theta_m(x) & \sin\theta_m(x)\  
-\sin\theta_m(x) & \cos\theta_m(x)  
\end{pmatrix}.  
$$

# Compute:  
$$  
\frac{dU_m}{dx}

\frac{d\theta_m}{dx}  
\begin{pmatrix}  
-\sin\theta_m & \cos\theta_m\  
-\cos\theta_m & -\sin\theta_m  
\end{pmatrix}.  
$$

# Since (U_m) is real orthogonal, (U_m^\dagger=U_m^T). Compute:  
$$  
U_m^\dagger\frac{dU_m}{dx}

\frac{d\theta_m}{dx}  
\begin{pmatrix}  
0 & 1\  
-1 & 0  
\end{pmatrix}.  
$$

# Therefore:  
$$  
-iU_m^\dagger\frac{dU_m}{dx}

\begin{pmatrix}  
0 & -i\frac{d\theta_m}{dx}\  
+i\frac{d\theta_m}{dx} & 0  
\end{pmatrix}.  
$$

### 8.5 Final “propagation in matter basis” equation (what the slide shows)

If:  
$$  
U_m^\dagger H_f U_m=  
\begin{pmatrix}  
\frac{m_{1m}^2}{2E} & 0\  
0 & \frac{m_{2m}^2}{2E}  
\end{pmatrix},  
$$

# then:  
$$  
i\frac{d}{dx}  
\begin{pmatrix}  
|\nu_{1m}\rangle\  
|\nu_{2m}\rangle  
\end{pmatrix}

\begin{pmatrix}  
\frac{m_{1m}^2}{2E} & -i\frac{d\theta_m}{dx}\  
+i\frac{d\theta_m}{dx} & \frac{m_{2m}^2}{2E}  
\end{pmatrix}  
\begin{pmatrix}  
|\nu_{1m}\rangle\  
|\nu_{2m}\rangle  
\end{pmatrix}.  
$$

Interpretation:

- The diagonal terms are phase accumulation in instantaneous eigenstates.
    
- The off-diagonal terms cause transitions between them when density changes.
    

Adiabatic condition (qualitative): if:  
$$  
\left|\frac{d\theta_m}{dx}\right|  
\ll  
\left|\frac{m_{2m}^2-m_{1m}^2}{2E}\right|,  
$$  
then transitions are suppressed (adiabatic).

Landau–Zener jumping probability (as quoted in lecture):  
$$  
P_L=\exp\left(-\frac{\pi}{2}\gamma\right).  
$$

## 9) Solar neutrino problem: vacuum vs MSW behaviour (useful “numbers”)

For the LMA-MSW solution, survival probability is energy-dependent.

High-energy solar neutrinos (adiabatic MSW, produced at high density) give approximately:  
$$  
P_{ee}^{\text{high }E}\simeq \sin^2\theta_{12}.  
$$

Low-energy solar neutrinos (vacuum oscillations averaged over many cycles) give approximately:  
$$  
P_{ee}^{\text{low }E}\simeq 1-\frac{1}{2}\sin^2(2\theta_{12}).  
$$

These are the two limits typically used in exam questions.

## 10) Mass ordering (hierarchy) and why it matters (JUNO + matter effects)

Known sign:  
$$  
\Delta m_{21}^2>0.  
$$

Unknown sign:  
$$  
\Delta m_{31}^2\ \text{(or equivalently the ordering of }m_3\text{)}.  
$$

Normal ordering:  
$$  
m_1<m_2<m_3.  
$$

Inverted ordering:  
$$  
m_3<m_1<m_2.  
$$

Why it matters:

- In matter, resonance depends on the sign in:  
    $$  
    A=\Delta m^2\cos 2\theta.  
    $$  
    So ordering controls whether resonant enhancement occurs for neutrinos vs antineutrinos in certain channels.
    
- In JUNO (reactor (\bar\nu_e)), the spectrum contains interference between oscillation frequencies set by:  
    $$  
    \Delta m_{21}^2,\ \Delta m_{31}^2,\ \Delta m_{32}^2,  
    $$  
    and the fine “wiggle pattern” differs slightly between normal and inverted ordering.

## 11) Baryon content of the Universe and why symmetry fails

Baryon-to-photon ratio:  
$$  
\eta \equiv \frac{n_b-n_{\bar b}}{n_\gamma}\simeq 6\times 10^{-10}.  
$$

Approximate baryon mass density:  
$$  
\rho_b \simeq m_p,n_b \simeq m_p,\eta,n_\gamma.  
$$

Critical density:  
$$  
\rho_{\text{cr}}=\frac{3H_0^2}{8\pi G}.  
$$

Baryon fraction:  
$$  
\Omega_b\equiv \frac{\rho_b}{\rho_{\text{cr}}}\sim 0.05.  
$$

Symmetric-universe annihilation idea:  
$$  
p+\bar p \leftrightarrow \gamma+\gamma.  
$$

Non-relativistic equilibrium number density scaling (Boltzmann suppression):  
$$  
n_p \propto \left(m_p T\right)^{3/2}e^{-m_p/T}.  
$$

Photon number density scaling:  
$$  
n_\gamma \propto T^3.  
$$

Therefore:  
$$  
\frac{n_p}{n_\gamma}\propto \left(\frac{m_p}{T}\right)^{3/2}e^{-m_p/T}.  
$$

At freeze-out temperatures well below (m_p), this becomes extremely small (order (10^{-19}) in the slide estimate), far below:  
$$  
\eta\sim 10^{-10}.  
$$

Conclusion: the Universe must generate a net asymmetry dynamically.

## 12) Sakharov conditions (why baryogenesis needs 3 ingredients)

To generate net baryon number from an initially symmetric state, you need:

1. Baryon number violation:  
    $$  
    \Delta B \neq 0.  
    $$
    
2. C and CP violation:  
    $$  
    \Gamma(\text{process})\neq \Gamma(\text{CP-conjugate process}).  
    $$
    
3. Departure from thermal equilibrium:  
    $$  
    \text{not in equilibrium so inverse reactions do not erase asymmetry}.  
    $$
    

Equilibrium washout idea (what the slide means by “equilibrium drives reverse reactions”): if equilibrium holds, detailed balance makes forward/backward processes cancel, preventing a net (B).

## 13) Leptogenesis (neutrino link to baryogenesis)

Introduce heavy right-handed (sterile) neutrinos (N). Decays:  
$$  
N\to \ell + H,  
\qquad  
N\to \bar\ell + H^\dagger.  
$$

Define CP asymmetry parameter:  
$$  
\varepsilon  
\equiv  
\frac{\Gamma(N\to \ell H)-\Gamma(N\to \bar\ell H^\dagger)}  
{\Gamma(N\to \ell H)+\Gamma(N\to \bar\ell H^\dagger)}.  
$$

Loop corrections + complex phases allow:  
$$  
\varepsilon\neq 0,  
$$  
creating a lepton asymmetry:  
$$  
L\neq 0.  
$$

At high temperature, electroweak sphalerons partially convert (L) into (B):  
$$  
L\to B.  
$$

Seesaw link (why neutrino masses appear in the same framework):  
$$  
m_\nu \sim \frac{y^2 v^2}{M_N}.  
$$
