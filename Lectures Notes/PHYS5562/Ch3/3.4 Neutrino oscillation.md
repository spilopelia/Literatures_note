## 0. The one-sentence idea

Neutrinos are produced/detected in **flavour states** $$|\nu_\alpha\rangle ;(\alpha=e,\mu,\tau)$$ but they propagate as **mass states** $$|\nu_i\rangle ;(i=1,2,3)$$ with slightly different phases, so a neutrino created as one flavour can later be detected as another.

---

## 1. Flavour vs mass eigenstates and the PMNS matrix

### 1.1 Definition of mixing

The lecture uses the convention (equivalent to what’s on the slides) that flavour states are linear combinations of mass eigenstates:

# $$  
\begin{pmatrix}  
|\nu_e\rangle\  
|\nu_\mu\rangle\  
|\nu_\tau\rangle  
\end{pmatrix}

U^*  
\begin{pmatrix}  
|\nu_1\rangle\  
|\nu_2\rangle\  
|\nu_3\rangle  
\end{pmatrix}.  
$$

Equivalently, component-wise:

$$  
|\nu_\alpha\rangle = \sum_{i=1}^3 U^*_{\alpha i},|\nu_i\rangle,  
\qquad  
|\nu_i\rangle = \sum_{\alpha=e,\mu,\tau} U_{\alpha i},|\nu_\alpha\rangle,  
$$

where $$U$$ is the PMNS mixing matrix (unitary: $$U^\dagger U = I$$).

### 1.2 Propagation is simple in the mass basis

Each mass eigenstate satisfies the free Schrödinger equation in time:

$$  
i\frac{d}{dt}|\nu_i(t)\rangle = H_i |\nu_i(t)\rangle,  
\qquad  
|\nu_i(t)\rangle = e^{-iH_i t}|\nu_i(0)\rangle.  
$$

So the key is: what is $$H_i$$?

---

## 2. Why the Hamiltonian becomes $$E + \frac{m^2}{2E}$$ (ultrarelativistic expansion)

The slide “Neutrino propagation” states:

$$  
H = E = \sqrt{p^2 + m^2}  
\simeq E + \frac{m^2}{2E}.  
$$

Here is the full derivation (the “general rule” is **binomial expansion** for small parameters).

### 2.1 Start from the exact relativistic energy

$$  
E = \sqrt{p^2+m^2}.  
$$

Factor out $$p$$:

$$  
E = p\sqrt{1+\frac{m^2}{p^2}}.  
$$

### 2.2 Use binomial expansion for small $$x$$

For $$|x|\ll 1$$:

$$  
(1+x)^{1/2} = 1 + \frac{x}{2} - \frac{x^2}{8} + \cdots  
$$

Let $$x=\frac{m^2}{p^2}$$. Neutrinos are ultrarelativistic in most oscillation experiments, so $$m^2\ll p^2$$, hence $$x\ll1$$. Keep only the first correction:

$$  
E \approx p\left(1+\frac{1}{2}\frac{m^2}{p^2}\right)  
= p + \frac{m^2}{2p}.  
$$

### 2.3 Replace $$p\simeq E$$ consistently

To leading order for ultrarelativistic particles, $$p\simeq E$$ (the difference is already order $$m^2/E$$). So

$$  
E \approx E + \frac{m^2}{2E}.  
$$

**Interpretation:** the huge common part $$E$$ is the same for all mass eigenstates (approximately), while the tiny part $$\frac{m_i^2}{2E}$$ differs between eigenstates and is what drives oscillations. This is exactly what is used in the time-evolution phase on the two-flavour slide:

## 3. Two-flavour vacuum oscillation: full derivation

The lecture derives (for 2 flavours):

$$  
P_{ee} = 1-\sin^2(2\theta),\sin^2!\left(\frac{\Delta m^2,L}{4E}\right).  
$$

Below is the complete derivation with every step explicit.

### 3.1 Two-flavour mixing definition

Define two flavour states $$|\nu_e\rangle,|\nu_\mu\rangle$$ and two mass states $$|\nu_1\rangle,|\nu_2\rangle$$ related by a rotation:

# $$  
\begin{pmatrix}  
|\nu_e\rangle\  
|\nu_\mu\rangle  
\end{pmatrix}

\begin{pmatrix}  
c & s\  
-s & c  
\end{pmatrix}  
\begin{pmatrix}  
|\nu_1\rangle\  
|\nu_2\rangle  
\end{pmatrix},  
\qquad  
c=\cos\theta,; s=\sin\theta.  
$$

So

$$  
|\nu_e\rangle = c|\nu_1\rangle + s|\nu_2\rangle,  
\qquad  
|\nu_\mu\rangle = -s|\nu_1\rangle + c|\nu_2\rangle.  
$$

### 3.2 Time evolution in the mass basis

Each mass eigenstate picks up a phase:

$$  
|\nu_i(t)\rangle = e^{-iE_i t},|\nu_i(0)\rangle.  
$$

Using the ultrarelativistic expansion from Section 2:

$$  
E_i \simeq E + \frac{m_i^2}{2E}.  
$$

So

# $$  
|\nu_i(t)\rangle

# e^{-i\left(E+\frac{m_i^2}{2E}\right)t},|\nu_i(0)\rangle

e^{-iEt},e^{-i\frac{m_i^2}{2E}t},|\nu_i(0)\rangle.  
$$

### 3.3 Evolve an initial electron neutrino

Assume at $$t=0$$ we create $$|\nu_e(0)\rangle$$:

$$  
|\nu_e(0)\rangle = c|\nu_1(0)\rangle + s|\nu_2(0)\rangle.  
$$

At time $$t$$:

# $$  
|\nu_e(t)\rangle

c|\nu_1(t)\rangle + s|\nu_2(t)\rangle  
$$

# $$

c,e^{-iEt}e^{-i\frac{m_1^2}{2E}t}|\nu_1(0)\rangle  
+  
s,e^{-iEt}e^{-i\frac{m_2^2}{2E}t}|\nu_2(0)\rangle.  
$$

Factor out the common phase $$e^{-iEt}$$:

# $$  
|\nu_e(t)\rangle

e^{-iEt}  
\left(  
c,e^{-i\frac{m_1^2}{2E}t}|\nu_1(0)\rangle  
+  
s,e^{-i\frac{m_2^2}{2E}t}|\nu_2(0)\rangle  
\right).  
$$

### 3.4 Convert time to baseline length

Neutrinos are ultra-relativistic, so $$t\simeq L$$ (in units $$c=1$$). The slide writes $$L=ct$$.

So

# $$  
|\nu_e(L)\rangle

e^{-iEL}  
\left(  
c,e^{-i\frac{m_1^2}{2E}L}|\nu_1(0)\rangle  
+  
s,e^{-i\frac{m_2^2}{2E}L}|\nu_2(0)\rangle  
\right).  
$$

Factor out $$e^{-i\frac{m_1^2}{2E}L}$$ as well:

# $$  
|\nu_e(L)\rangle

e^{-iEL},e^{-i\frac{m_1^2}{2E}L}  
\left(  
c|\nu_1(0)\rangle  
+  
s,e^{-i\frac{(m_2^2-m_1^2)}{2E}L}|\nu_2(0)\rangle  
\right).  
$$

Define

$$  
\Delta m^2 \equiv m_2^2-m_1^2,  
\qquad  
\phi \equiv \frac{\Delta m^2,L}{2E}.  
$$

Then

# $$  
|\nu_e(L)\rangle

\text{(overall phase)}  
\left(  
c|\nu_1\rangle  
+  
s,e^{-i\phi}|\nu_2\rangle  
\right).  
$$

### 3.5 General rule: overall phases do not affect probabilities

Probabilities use absolute squares like $$|\langle\nu_e|\nu_e(L)\rangle|^2$$, so any global phase cancels. That’s the same “identity matrix / common term doesn’t affect transition probability” idea emphasized later in the flavour-Hamiltonian slide.

So we can drop the overall phase and focus on:

$$  
|\tilde\nu_e(L)\rangle = c|\nu_1\rangle + s e^{-i\phi}|\nu_2\rangle.  
$$

### 3.6 Compute the survival amplitude and probability

The bra:

$$  
\langle \nu_e| = c\langle\nu_1| + s\langle\nu_2|.  
$$

So the amplitude:

# $$  
A_{ee}(L) \equiv \langle\nu_e|\tilde\nu_e(L)\rangle

(c\langle\nu_1|+s\langle\nu_2|)(c|\nu_1\rangle + s e^{-i\phi}|\nu_2\rangle).  
$$

Use orthonormality $$\langle\nu_i|\nu_j\rangle=\delta_{ij}$$:

$$  
A_{ee}(L) = c^2 + s^2 e^{-i\phi}.  
$$

Then

$$  
P_{ee}(L) = |A_{ee}(L)|^2  
= (c^2 + s^2 e^{-i\phi})(c^2 + s^2 e^{+i\phi})  
$$

$$  
= c^4 + s^4 + c^2 s^2(e^{i\phi}+e^{-i\phi})  
= c^4 + s^4 + 2c^2 s^2 \cos\phi.  
$$

Now rewrite $$c^4+s^4 = (c^2+s^2)^2 - 2c^2 s^2 = 1 - 2c^2 s^2$$, so

$$  
P_{ee}(L) = 1 - 2c^2 s^2 + 2c^2 s^2\cos\phi  
= 1 - 2c^2 s^2(1-\cos\phi).  
$$

Use the identity $$1-\cos\phi = 2\sin^2(\phi/2)$$:

$$  
P_{ee}(L) = 1 - 4c^2 s^2 \sin^2\left(\frac{\phi}{2}\right).  
$$

Finally, $$4c^2 s^2 = \sin^2(2\theta)$$ and $$\phi/2 = \frac{\Delta m^2 L}{4E}$$, giving

$$  
P_{ee}(L)=1-\sin^2(2\theta),\sin^2\left(\frac{\Delta m^2L}{4E}\right),  
$$

which matches the slide formula.

## 4. Oscillation Hamiltonian in the flavour basis and the “identity term” rule

The deck explicitly notes: adding an identity-matrix piece does not affect transition probabilities.

### 4.1 Why?

If

$$  
H \rightarrow H + \lambda I,  
$$

then the evolution operator becomes

$$  
e^{-i(H+\lambda I)t} = e^{-i\lambda t},e^{-iHt}.  
$$

The extra factor $$e^{-i\lambda t}$$ is a **global phase**, and when you compute probabilities

$$  
P_{\alpha\beta} = |\langle\nu_\beta|e^{-iHt}|\nu_\alpha\rangle|^2,  
$$

that phase cancels.

**Practical consequence:** only **energy differences** matter, which is why oscillations depend on $$\Delta m^2$$ rather than absolute masses.

## 5. Matter effect (MSW): what each term means and how $$\theta_m$$ is obtained

The key slide in flavour basis (2 flavours) is:

# $$  
i\frac{d}{dt}  
\begin{pmatrix}  
|\nu_e\rangle\  
|\nu_\mu\rangle  
\end{pmatrix}

\left[  
\frac{\Delta m^2}{4E}  
\begin{pmatrix}  
-\cos 2\theta & \sin 2\theta\  
\sin 2\theta & \cos 2\theta  
\end{pmatrix}  
+  
\begin{pmatrix}  
2G_F N_e & 0\  
0 & 0  
\end{pmatrix}  
\right]  
\begin{pmatrix}  
|\nu_e\rangle\  
|\nu_\mu\rangle  
\end{pmatrix}.  
$$

_(The deck also defines a convenient symbol $$A = 2E\times 2G_F N_e$$ on the same slide.)_

### 5.1 Meaning of each object

- $$|\nu_e\rangle,|\nu_\mu\rangle$$: flavour states (what weak interactions create/detect).
    
- $$E$$: neutrino energy (approximately common to the two mass states).
    
- $$\Delta m^2 = m_2^2-m_1^2$$: vacuum mass-squared splitting.
    
- $$\theta$$: vacuum mixing angle between flavour and mass bases.
    
- $$G_F$$: Fermi constant (strength of weak interaction).
    
- $$N_e$$: electron number density in matter.
    
- The first matrix (proportional to $$\Delta m^2/4E$$) is the **vacuum oscillation Hamiltonian** written in the flavour basis.
    
- The second matrix is the **matter potential**: only $$\nu_e$$ feels an extra charged-current forward-scattering potential in ordinary matter (because matter contains electrons but essentially no muons/taus). The slide derives an averaged effective potential proportional to $$2G_F N_e$$ and notes the sign changes for antineutrinos.
    

### 5.2 Why the matter term is diagonal

Forward scattering in matter does not change the neutrino flavour in the scattering process; it just shifts the effective energy of $$\nu_e$$ relative to $$\nu_\mu$$. That’s why it appears as a diagonal contribution:

$$  
V =  
\begin{pmatrix}  
V_e & 0\  
0 & V_\mu  
\end{pmatrix},  
\qquad  
V_e = 2G_F N_e,;;V_\mu\simeq 0.  
$$

Only the **difference** $$V_e-V_\mu$$ matters (again the identity-term rule).

### 5.3 Introducing $$A$$ and rewriting the Hamiltonian

The slide rewrites the total Hamiltonian as one matrix with a common $$1/(4E)$$ factor:

Define

$$  
A \equiv 2E \times 2G_F N_e.  
$$

Then

# $$  
\begin{pmatrix}  
2G_F N_e & 0\  
0 & 0  
\end{pmatrix}

\frac{1}{4E}  
\begin{pmatrix}  
2A & 0\  
0 & 0  
\end{pmatrix}.  
$$

So the total becomes

# $$  
H_f

\frac{1}{4E}  
\begin{pmatrix}  
-\Delta m^2\cos 2\theta + 2A & \Delta m^2\sin 2\theta\  
\Delta m^2\sin 2\theta & \Delta m^2\cos 2\theta  
\end{pmatrix}.  
$$

### 5.4 Does this equation “give” $$\theta_m$$?

This equation defines the **Hamiltonian in matter**. To get $$\theta_m$$ you must **diagonalize** this matrix. The diagonalization angle is _by definition_ the matter mixing angle $$\theta_m$$.

That is exactly what the deck says: “Diagonalize the matrix… The rotation matrix defines a mixing angle between flavour and mass eigenstates.”

### 5.5 Diagonalization and the formula for $$\tan 2\theta_m$$

For any real symmetric $$2\times2$$ matrix

$$  
M=  
\begin{pmatrix}  
a & b\  
b & d  
\end{pmatrix},  
$$

a rotation by angle $$\theta_m$$ diagonalizes it, with the general rule

$$  
\tan(2\theta_m)=\frac{2b}{d-a}.  
$$

Apply this to the matter Hamiltonian matrix (ignoring the overall $$1/(4E)$$ factor since it does not affect eigenvectors):

- $$a = -\Delta m^2\cos 2\theta + 2A$$
    
- $$d = \Delta m^2\cos 2\theta$$
    
- $$b = \Delta m^2\sin 2\theta$$
    

Compute

# $$  
d-a

# \Delta m^2\cos 2\theta -(-\Delta m^2\cos 2\theta + 2A)

2(\Delta m^2\cos 2\theta - A).  
$$

Then

# $$  
\tan(2\theta_m)

# \frac{2(\Delta m^2\sin 2\theta)}{2(\Delta m^2\cos 2\theta - A)}

\frac{\Delta m^2\sin 2\theta}{\Delta m^2\cos 2\theta - A},  
$$

which matches the slide.

### 5.6 Matter-modified mass splitting $$\Delta m_m^2$$

The eigenvalue separation for $$M$$ is

$$  
\Delta\lambda = \sqrt{(d-a)^2 + (2b)^2}.  
$$

Using the same substitutions:

# $$  
\Delta m_m^2

\sqrt{(\Delta m^2\cos 2\theta - A)^2 + (\Delta m^2\sin 2\theta)^2},  
$$

also shown on the deck.

### 5.7 Resonance condition (MSW enhancement)

Mixing is maximal when $$\theta_m=\pi/4$$, i.e. when $$\tan(2\theta_m)\to\infty$$, which requires

$$  
\Delta m^2\cos 2\theta - A = 0  
\quad\Rightarrow\quad  
A = \Delta m^2\cos 2\theta,  
$$

as stated on the slide.

## 6. Varying density: adiabatic evolution and the Landau–Zener term

When density changes with position, $$\theta_m=\theta_m(x)$$ varies. The lecture shows that when you move to the _instantaneous matter eigenstate basis_ $$|\nu_{1m}\rangle,|\nu_{2m}\rangle$$ you get extra off-diagonal terms proportional to $$d\theta_m/dx$$.

### 6.1 What that equation means physically

- The diagonal terms $$\sim m_{1m}^2/(2E),; m_{2m}^2/(2E)$$ are the usual phase accumulation in the instantaneous eigenstates.
    
- The off-diagonal terms $$\propto i,d\theta_m/dx$$ mix the instantaneous eigenstates when the medium changes.
    

So:

- **Slow density change** (small $$|d\theta_m/dx|$$) → the neutrino _stays_ on its instantaneous eigenstate (“adiabatic”, no level crossing).
    
- **Rapid density change** → higher chance to jump between eigenstates (“non-adiabatic”, level crossing).
    

### 6.2 Landau–Zener crossing probability

The slide quotes a Landau–Zener-type probability:

$$  
P_L = \exp\left(-\frac{\pi}{2}\gamma\right),  
\qquad  
\gamma = \frac{\delta m^2 \sin^2 2\theta}{2E\cos 2\theta,(1/n_e),(dn_e/dr)}.  
$$

Interpretation:

- $$P_L$$ is the probability to “jump” at the resonance region (level crossing).
    
- Larger $$\gamma$$ (more adiabatic) → smaller $$P_L$$.
    
- Faster density gradient $$|dn_e/dr|$$ → smaller $$\gamma$$ → larger $$P_L$$ (more jumping).

## 8. Three flavours: degrees of freedom and why the CP phase is put in $$R_{13}$$

### 8.1 Degrees of freedom counting (why “4 parameters left”)

The slide’s logic is: a general complex $$3\times3$$ matrix has 18 real parameters; unitarity constraints reduce them; then unphysical phases are removed, leaving 4 physical parameters.

A clean way to state it:

1. A general unitary $$3\times3$$ matrix has $$9$$ real parameters.
    
2. For **Dirac** neutrinos, you can rephase charged leptons and neutrinos:
    
    - 3 charged lepton phases (e, μ, τ)
        
    - 3 neutrino flavour phases
        
    - but 1 overall common phase is unobservable  
        → removable phases = $$3+3-1=5$$ (matches the slide “5 phases”).
        
3. So physical parameters in mixing relevant to oscillations:  
    $$9-5=4 = 3\ \text{angles} + 1\ \text{CP phase}.$$
    

This matches the summary slide: “3 angles… 1 CP violating phase”.

### 8.2 Standard PMNS parameterization

A standard choice is

$$  
U = R_{23}(\theta_{23});R_{13}(\theta_{13},\delta);R_{12}(\theta_{12}),  
$$

with

$$  
R_{12}=  
\begin{pmatrix}  
c_{12} & s_{12} & 0\  
-s_{12} & c_{12} & 0\  
0 & 0 & 1  
\end{pmatrix},  
\quad  
R_{23}=  
\begin{pmatrix}  
1 & 0 & 0\  
0 & c_{23} & s_{23}\  
0 & -s_{23} & c_{23}  
\end{pmatrix},  
$$

and the complex rotation

$$  
R_{13}=  
\begin{pmatrix}  
c_{13} & 0 & s_{13}e^{-i\delta}\  
0 & 1 & 0\  
-s_{13}e^{i\delta} & 0 & c_{13}  
\end{pmatrix},  
\qquad  
c_{ij}=\cos\theta_{ij},; s_{ij}=\sin\theta_{ij}.  
$$

### 8.3 Why do we “define” $$R_{13}$$ to carry the CP phase?

This is **a convention**, chosen because:

1. **Only one physical Dirac phase exists**, so we can place it anywhere convenient by rephasing fields.
    
2. Putting $$\delta$$ in $$R_{13}$$ makes CP violation disappear automatically if $$\theta_{13}=0$$:
    
    - If $$s_{13}=0$$, then $$R_{13}$$ becomes real and $$\delta$$ drops out completely.
        
    - This matches physics: with $$\theta_{13}=0$$, the 3-flavour system effectively reduces to two decoupled 2-flavour mixings and you cannot get genuine 3-flavour CP violation.
        
3. In long-baseline appearance channels, CP-odd effects typically require interference involving the “1–3” sector, so packaging $$\delta$$ there makes formulas and interpretation cleaner.
    

So: **the reason is not that Nature prefers 1–3**, but that **rephasing freedom** lets us move the single physical phase into a convenient spot, and $$R_{13}$$ is the standard convenient spot.

## 9. General 3-flavour probability and how it decomposes into Re/Im terms

The slide gives the final decomposition:

# $$  
P_{\alpha\beta}

\delta_{\alpha\beta}  
-4\sum_{i<j}\mathrm{Re}!\left[U_{\alpha i}U^__{\beta i}U^__{\alpha j}U_{\beta j}\right]\sin^2 X_{ij}  
+2\sum_{i<j}\mathrm{Im}!\left[U_{\alpha i}U^__{\beta i}U^__{\alpha j}U_{\beta j}\right]\sin 2X_{ij},  
$$

with

$$  
X_{ij}=\frac{\Delta m_{ij}^2,L}{4E}.  
$$

Here is the full derivation.

### 9.1 Start from the amplitude

Create flavour $$\alpha$$ at $$0$$:

$$  
|\nu_\alpha(0)\rangle = \sum_i U^*_{\alpha i}|\nu_i\rangle.  
$$

After propagation (vacuum):

$$  
|\nu_\alpha(L)\rangle = \sum_i U^*_{\alpha i}e^{-i\frac{m_i^2}{2E}L}|\nu_i\rangle  
$$

(drop the common $$e^{-iEL}$$ global phase as in Section 3).

Project onto $$|\nu_\beta\rangle = \sum_j U^*_{\beta j}|\nu_j\rangle$$:

# $$  
A_{\alpha\beta}  
\equiv \langle \nu_\beta|\nu_\alpha(L)\rangle

\sum_{i,j} U_{\beta j},U^*_{\alpha i},e^{-i\frac{m_i^2}{2E}L}\langle \nu_j|\nu_i\rangle.  
$$

Since $$\langle\nu_j|\nu_i\rangle=\delta_{ji}$$, only $$j=i$$ survives:

# $$  
A_{\alpha\beta}

\sum_i U_{\beta i},U^*_{\alpha i},e^{-i\frac{m_i^2}{2E}L}.  
$$

### 9.2 Square the amplitude

# $$  
P_{\alpha\beta} = |A_{\alpha\beta}|^2

\left(\sum_i U_{\beta i}U^__{\alpha i}e^{-i\phi_i}\right)  
\left(\sum_j U^__{\beta j}U_{\alpha j}e^{+i\phi_j}\right),  
$$

where

$$  
\phi_i \equiv \frac{m_i^2L}{2E}.  
$$

Expand:

$$  
P_{\alpha\beta}
\sum_{i,j}  
U_{\beta i}U^__{\alpha i}U^__{\beta j}U_{\alpha j}  
e^{-i(\phi_i-\phi_j)}.  
$$

Define the phase difference:

# $$  
\phi_i-\phi_j = \frac{(m_i^2-m_j^2)L}{2E}

\frac{\Delta m_{ij}^2 L}{2E}.  
$$

Also define the product (often called a “quartet”):

$$  
J^{ij}_{\alpha\beta} \equiv  
U_{\alpha i}U^__{\beta i}U^__{\alpha j}U_{\beta j}.  
$$

Then

$$  
P_{\alpha\beta} = \sum_{i,j} J^{ij}_{\alpha\beta},e^{-i\frac{\Delta m_{ij}^2L}{2E}}.  
$$

### 9.3 Separate diagonal and off-diagonal pieces

When $$i=j$$, $$\Delta m_{ii}^2=0$$ and $$e^{0}=1$$:

# $$  
\sum_i J^{ii}_{\alpha\beta}

\sum_i |U_{\alpha i}|^2|U_{\beta i}|^2.  
$$

Using unitarity, one can show that the full expression becomes

# $$  
P_{\alpha\beta}

\delta_{\alpha\beta}  
+  
\sum_{i\neq j} J^{ij}_{\alpha\beta},e^{-i\frac{\Delta m_{ij}^2L}{2E}}.  
$$

Now pair terms $$ij$$ with $$ji$$. Note:

$$  
J^{ji}_{\alpha\beta} = (J^{ij}_{\alpha\beta})^*,  
\qquad  
\Delta m_{ji}^2 = -\Delta m_{ij}^2.  
$$

So the pair contribution is

$$  
J^{ij}e^{-i\Delta} + (J^{ij})^* e^{+i\Delta}  
= 2,\mathrm{Re}(J^{ij}e^{-i\Delta}).  
$$

Write $$J^{ij}=\mathrm{Re}J^{ij}+i,\mathrm{Im}J^{ij}$$:

# $$  
\mathrm{Re}(J^{ij}e^{-i\Delta})

\mathrm{Re}J^{ij}\cos\Delta  
+\mathrm{Im}J^{ij}\sin\Delta.  
$$

Now use trig identities:

$$  
\cos\Delta = 1-2\sin^2\left(\frac{\Delta}{2}\right),  
\qquad  
\sin\Delta = \sin(2X),  
$$

with

$$  
X \equiv \frac{\Delta}{2} = \frac{\Delta m_{ij}^2L}{4E}.  
$$

Putting everything together gives:

# $$  
P_{\alpha\beta}

\delta_{\alpha\beta}  
-4\sum_{i<j}\mathrm{Re}(J^{ij}_{\alpha\beta})\sin^2 X_{ij}  
+2\sum_{i<j}\mathrm{Im}(J^{ij}_{\alpha\beta})\sin(2X_{ij}),  
$$

which is exactly the slide result.

## 10. CP in oscillations: what the slide is emphasizing

The slide states that the “first line is CP conserving” while the “second line violates CP if $$U$$ is complex.”

### 10.1 What changes under CP?

In vacuum, CP effectively complex-conjugates the mixing matrix in the oscillation amplitude. The slide writes:

$$  
|\nu_\alpha\rangle = U^*|\nu_i\rangle,  
\qquad  
|\bar\nu_\alpha\rangle = U|\bar\nu_i\rangle.  
$$

So neutrino probabilities use $$U$$, antineutrino probabilities use $$U^*$$.

### 10.2 Why only the imaginary term is CP-odd

If you replace $$U\to U^*$$, then

- $$\mathrm{Re}(J^{ij}_{\alpha\beta})$$ stays the same,
    
- $$\mathrm{Im}(J^{ij}_{\alpha\beta})$$ flips sign.
    

Therefore:

- The $$-4\sum \mathrm{Re}[\cdots]\sin^2X$$ term is the same for $$\nu$$ and $$\bar\nu$$ → CP-even.
    
- The $$+2\sum \mathrm{Im}[\cdots]\sin 2X$$ term changes sign → CP-odd.
    

That is the clean mathematical meaning of the slide’s bullets.

### 10.3 What the slide means by comparing probabilities

The slide compares (schematically):

- $$P(\nu_{e,L}\to\nu_{\mu,L})$$
    
- $$P(\bar\nu_{e,R}\to\bar\nu_{\mu,R})$$
    

and says they can differ if $$U$$ is complex, implying CP violation.

Why left-handed vs right-handed? Because weak interactions only couple to:

- left-handed neutrinos
    
- right-handed antineutrinos
    

So those are the physically produced/detected states.

## 11. Mass hierarchy: why it matters (physics + experiments)

The deck highlights that one “mass hierarchy” sign is unknown.

### 11.1 What “hierarchy” means

We know two independent splittings:

$$  
\Delta m_{21}^2 \equiv m_2^2-m_1^2 \quad (\text{solar, known } >0),  
$$

and one “atmospheric-scale” splitting, often taken as $$\Delta m_{31}^2$$ or $$\Delta m_{32}^2$$, whose sign determines the ordering:

- **Normal hierarchy (NH):** $$m_3$$ is heaviest → $$\Delta m_{31}^2>0$$
    
- **Inverted hierarchy (IH):** $$m_3$$ is lightest → $$\Delta m_{31}^2<0$$
    

### 11.2 Why the sign matters even if oscillations depend on $$\Delta m^2$$

- In **vacuum**, probabilities depend on $$\sin^2(\Delta m^2 L/4E)$$, so a single-frequency two-flavour channel is insensitive to the sign.
    
- In **three flavours**, multiple frequencies interfere; detailed spectral patterns can differ subtly with ordering.
    
- In **matter**, the MSW resonance condition involves $$A=\Delta m^2\cos 2\theta$$, so the sign of the relevant $$\Delta m^2$$ determines whether resonance happens for neutrinos or antineutrinos (and at what energies). This is a direct physical sensitivity to hierarchy.
    

### 11.3 Why JUNO cares (reactor spectrum interference)

JUNO aims to resolve the hierarchy by extremely precise measurement of the reactor $$\bar\nu_e$$ energy spectrum: the tiny “wiggles” come from interference between oscillations driven by solar and atmospheric splittings, and the phase pattern depends on whether the ordering is normal or inverted (the slide’s NH/IH curves differ).

## 12. Cosmology connection: baryons, symmetry, and why “something happened”

### 12.1 Baryon fraction today

The lecture lists the baryon-to-photon asymmetry and baryon density fraction:

$$  
\eta \simeq 6\times 10^{-10},  
$$

$$  
\rho_b \simeq m_p,\eta,n_\gamma \simeq 2.4\times 10^{-7},  
$$

$$  
\rho_{cr} \simeq 1.05 h^2 \times 10^{-5};{\rm GeV,cm^{-3}},  
\qquad  
\Omega_b \sim 0.05.  
$$

Meaning:

- $$\eta$$ measures how many more baryons than antibaryons survived per photon.
    
- $$\Omega_b$$ says baryons are only ~5% of the critical density (the rest is dark matter + dark energy in standard cosmology).
    

### 12.2 Why a perfectly symmetric universe fails

The “Symmetric Universe” slide considers proton–antiproton annihilation:

$$  
\bar p + p ;\leftrightarrow; \gamma + \gamma,  
$$

and shows that after freeze-out one expects an exponentially tiny leftover:

$$  
\frac{n_p}{n_\gamma} \sim \frac{n_{\bar p}}{n_\gamma}  
\sim \left(\frac{m_p}{T}\right)^{3/2} e^{-m_p/T} \sim 10^{-19}.  
$$

But observed is $$\eta\sim 10^{-10}$$, vastly larger. So the universe cannot have stayed perfectly matter–antimatter symmetric: a net asymmetry must have been generated.

### 12.3 “Baryogenesis” slide meaning

The “Baryogenesis” slide says: inflation makes the universe flat/homogeneous/symmetric, then “something happened”, and by BBN (~MeV) the universe is asymmetric.

That “something” is baryogenesis (or leptogenesis feeding baryogenesis): a dynamical process that generates $$\eta\neq 0$$.

## 13. Sakharov conditions (why these three are required)

The slide lists the Sakharov conditions:

### 13.1 Condition 1: Baryon number violation

If baryon number $$B$$ were exactly conserved, you could never change net baryon number from zero to nonzero.

The slide contrasts ordinary weak reactions like

$$  
n+\nu_e \to p^+ + e^-,  
$$

which **conserve** baryon number (both sides have $$B=1$$), with the _required_ idea of processes that effectively create net baryon number:

$$  
N \to B.  
$$

(Here $$N$$ is a heavy particle species; the schematic means “decays produce baryons in a way that changes net $$B$$”.)

### 13.2 Condition 2: C and CP violation

If C and CP were exact symmetries, matter and antimatter would be produced at identical rates. The slide encodes this as unequal decay rates:

$$  
\Gamma_N \neq \Gamma_{\bar N},  
\qquad  
N\to B \neq \bar N \to \bar B,  
$$

and similarly for chiral channels (L/R). If CP were conserved, total rates would balance and no net asymmetry survives.

**Connection to neutrino oscillations:** a complex PMNS matrix (nonzero $$\delta$$) is one experimentally accessible form of leptonic CP violation, which motivates leptogenesis scenarios (next section). (Oscillation CP violation is not automatically sufficient for baryogenesis, but it is a strong clue that CP violation exists in the lepton sector.)

### 13.3 Condition 3: Out of thermal equilibrium

The slide says thermal equilibrium drives the reverse reactions $$B\to N$$.

In equilibrium, detailed balance forces forward and backward rates to cancel, washing out net asymmetries. So you need decays or phase transitions happening out of equilibrium.

## 14. Leptogenesis: how neutrinos can generate the baryon asymmetry

The lecture’s leptogenesis slide summarizes: heavy right-handed neutrino decay (out-of-equilibrium), loop corrections that break lepton number and CP, and high-temperature SM processes converting $$L\to B$$.

### 14.1 Core mechanism (conceptual)

1. Introduce heavy right-handed neutrinos $$N$$ (not in the minimal SM).
    
2. They decay:
    

$$  
N \to \ell + H,  
\qquad  
N \to \bar\ell + H^\dagger,  
$$

where $$\ell$$ is a lepton doublet and $$H$$ the Higgs.

3. Tree-level and loop-level amplitudes interfere; if there are complex phases, the decay rates differ:
    

$$  
\Gamma(N\to \ell H) \neq \Gamma(N\to \bar\ell H^\dagger),  
$$

creating a net lepton asymmetry $$L\neq 0$$.

4. Electroweak sphaleron processes (high-temperature SM nonperturbative effects) violate $$B+L$$ but conserve $$B-L$$, partially converting that lepton asymmetry into baryon asymmetry:
    

$$  
L ;\to; B.  
$$

### 14.2 Seesaw link (why neutrino mass appears here)

The slide notes “Neutrinos Mass via seesaw mechanism.”

In type-I seesaw, adding heavy Majorana $$N$$ gives light neutrino masses of order

$$  
m_\nu \sim \frac{y^2 v^2}{M_N},  
$$

so the existence of tiny neutrino masses is naturally connected to the heavy states that can drive leptogenesis.

### 14.3 “One right-handed neutrino is keV could be dark matter”

The slide notes this possibility.  
Idea: a sterile/right-handed neutrino at keV scale can be long-lived and act as warm dark matter in some models.

## 15. What we know vs don’t know (exam-style checklist)

From the deck summary:

### Known (from oscillation data)

- Three mixing angles $$\theta_{12},\theta_{23},\theta_{13}$$ (now measured).
    
- Two independent mass-squared splittings $$\Delta m_{21}^2$$ and $$|\Delta m_{31}^2|$$.
    

### Unknown / not fully resolved

## 16. Quick “what to do” workflow for typical problems

### Vacuum, 2 flavours

1. Use mixing rotation with $$\theta$$.
    
2. Propagate phases $$e^{-im_i^2L/(2E)}$$.
    
3. Drop global phases (identity-term rule).
    
4. Compute probability.
    

### Matter, 2 flavours

1. Write flavour-basis Hamiltonian (vacuum + matter potential).
    
2. Diagonalize → get $$\theta_m$$ and $$\Delta m_m^2$$.
    
3. If density varies, check adiabatic vs nonadiabatic using $$d\theta_m/dx$$ and Landau–Zener $$P_L$$.
    

### 3 flavours + CP

1. Use amplitude $$A_{\alpha\beta}=\sum_i U_{\beta i}U^*_{\alpha i}e^{-im_i^2L/(2E)}$$.
    
2. Expand $$|A|^2$$ → Re/Im decomposition.
    
3. CP violation shows up in the imaginary term (requires complex $$U$$).
